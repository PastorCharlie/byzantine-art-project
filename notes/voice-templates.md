## The Three Brand-Voice Pillars

GLM's theological identity is calibrated across three doctrinal axes. Each axis has a corpus reference template — a specific reflection where the position was articulated with the right precision. Apply these templates whenever iconography forces the question.

### Pillar 1 — Eschatological (Post-Tribulation)

**Position:** Christ's return is one event, post-tribulation, visible, bodily, public. Rapture, resurrection, and judgment all occur at the parousia, not in stages. No secret pre-trib return.

**Reference templates:**
- **#16 Torcello Last Judgment:** *"Matthew 24:29 is explicit: 'Immediately after the tribulation of those days... they shall see the Son of man coming in the clouds of heaven with power and great glory.' After. Not before."*
- **#37 Hagia Sophia Thessaloniki Ascension:** *"This same Jesus, which is taken up from you into heaven, shall so come in like manner as ye have seen him go into heaven... There is no secret return. Acts 1:11 forbids it."*

**Apply when:** Last Judgment imagery, parousia themes, Anastasis, Heavenly Jerusalem, watchfulness scenes, tribulation references.

**Avoid:** pre-trib language, dispensational two-stage return, "any-moment" rapture framings, secret-rapture language.

**Anastasis-iconography standing rule (locked at #110+2, anchored at #111 Novgorod Anastasis):** for any future Anastasis entry (Christ raising Adam from broken doors of hell), the doctrinal anchor is **1 Corinthians 15:22** (*"as in Adam all die, even so in Christ shall all be made alive"*) — the Second-Adam-reverses-First-Adam gesture is what the iconography renders. The two-horizon Anastasis frame (already-accomplished resurrection-victory + yet-to-come universal resurrection at the parousia) is the standing Coll 3 reading. **1 Peter 3:18–19** (*"by which also he went and preached unto the spirits in prison"*) **may be referenced briefly but is not adjudicated.** The corpus does not take a side on the contested harrowing-of-hades exegesis — preached when, to whom, with what effect — because the apostolic text does not settle these questions and the iconography does not require them. Future Anastasis entries pattern-match to this restraint.

### Pillar 2 — Sacramental (Memorial View)

**Position:** GLM holds the memorial view of the Lord's Supper. The bread and cup proclaim Christ's death until he comes (1 Cor 11:26). The Spirit, not the elements, mediates Christ's presence. We do not hold transubstantiation, consubstantiation, or Reformed real-spiritual-presence in the metaphysical sense.

**Reference template:**
- **#30 Hosios Loukas apse Theotokos:** *"GLM holds the memorial view. The bread and cup proclaim Christ's death until he comes (1 Cor 11:26), and the Spirit, not the elements, mediates his presence. We do not hold that the Eucharist contains Christ's body in the metaphysical sense the Byzantines and the Catholics do. But we agree with the apse mosaic on the deeper claim..."*

**Three-step move:**
1. Identify the architectural-iconographic claim
2. Distinguish GLM's memorial view from the Byzantine/Catholic real-presence view
3. Affirm the shared foundational claim (the incarnation is real; the table presupposes it)

**Apply when:** Eucharistic imagery, Theotokos-over-altar, Communion of the Apostles, heavenly liturgy, Last Supper / Mystic Supper scenes.

**Avoid:** "Eucharist" as the primary term where "communion table" or "the bread and cup" works. Reserved-host / processional-host references. Any framing that makes the elements mediating instead of the Spirit.

### Pillar 3 — Pneumatological (Continuationist-Charismatic)

**Position:** GLM is Spirit-led, charismatic, non-denominational. The gifts of the Spirit poured out at Pentecost continue today. The Holy Spirit is given without measure to those who ask in faith. **Threads the needle:** rejects cessationism (which denies continuing gifts) and rejects prosperity-gospel/name-it-and-claim-it excess (which makes the Spirit transactional or guaranteed).

**Reference template:**
- **#35 Hosios Loukas Pentecost:** *"GLM is a Spirit-led ministry, and we read this mosaic as a literal record. The Spirit was poured out. The Spirit is still being poured out. The same fire that fell on the apostles' heads in Acts 2 falls on believers' heads in every generation that asks for it."*

**Key phrase:** *"every generation that asks for it"* — keeps the gift available without making it transactional or guaranteed.

**Apply when:** Pentecost iconography, baptism-of-Christ scenes (descent of Spirit), prophet-portraits (Spirit-empowered ministry), Spirit-related imagery generally.

**Avoid:** Cessationist framing ("the gifts ceased with the apostles"), prosperity-gospel framing ("claim your healing"), guarantee language, anything that makes Spirit-baptism a spiritual elite-marker.

## The Protoevangelium "Name and Pivot" Pattern (Locked-In Pattern, supplementary)

When iconography draws from the Protoevangelium of James or other apocryphal infancy gospels:

1. **Name** the apocryphal source openly
2. **Reject** canonical authority for the source
3. **Pivot** to canonical scripture that the iconographic detail accidentally illuminates
4. **Apply** pastorally without smuggling apocryphal narrative into Protestant doctrine

**Reference templates:**
- **#29 Sinai Annunciation:** veil-weaving (Protoevangelium) → Hebrews 10:20 (Christ's flesh as the veil)
- **#32 Daphni Presentation:** Mary's temple childhood (Protoevangelium) → John 2:19–21 (Christ as temple)
- **#36 Daphni Nativity:** nuanced — distinguishes (a) authentic mid-2nd-c. tradition (cave, Justin Martyr c. 150), (b) Protoevangelium fabrication (midwives), (c) canonical detail amplified by Protoevangelium (Joseph's doubt)

**Sites where this pattern will recur:** any Mary's-childhood scene, Joachim/Anne iconography, Dormition scenes, Nativity scenes (cave/midwives/Joseph's doubt), Mary's death/burial.

## OT Typology Rules (Collection 6 specific — locked in by Pastor Charlie before batch 008)

The corpus affirms typological readings of the Old Testament — but only when a New Testament author affirms the type first. Don't invent typology; receive it from the apostolic witness.

**Safe types** (NT-affirmed):
- Melchizedek as type of Christ (Hebrews 5–7)
- Christ as the true Passover lamb (1 Cor 5:7)
- Bronze serpent as type of Christ on the cross (John 3:14)
- Adam–Christ typology (Romans 5; 1 Cor 15:22, 45)
- Jonah's three days as type of resurrection (Matt 12:40)
- Israel's exodus as type of Christian deliverance (1 Cor 10:1–11)
- Tabernacle/temple as type of Christ's body and the heavenly sanctuary (Hebrews 9; John 2:21)
- Manna as type of the Bread of Life (John 6)

**Unsafe extrapolation** (avoid):
- Reading Christ into every OT detail (Origenist allegorism)
- Asserting OT figures as types when the NT only mentions the figure historically
- Marian typology drawn from Mary-as-Ark, Mary-as-burning-bush, etc., where the NT does not affirm
- Trinitarian readings of Genesis 18 (the three visitors at Mamre), since Genesis 19:1 explicitly identifies two of them as angels
- Any typology that requires assuming what the NT does not state

**Two failure modes to avoid:**
- **Allegorism** (Origenist): finding Christ in every iconographic detail through fanciful interpretation decoupled from the text's plain sense
- **Anti-typology** (dispensationalist): rejecting typological reading entirely because it threatens the Israel/Church distinction

GLM lands in the middle: the apostles' typological hermeneutic is the model. Hebrews on Melchizedek; John 3 on the bronze serpent; 1 Cor 10 on the wilderness — these are how typology is done correctly. Don't go past where the NT goes.

**Rublev's Hospitality of Abraham (Coll 6 flagship):**

Orthodox tradition reads the three visitors at Mamre as a Trinitarian appearance. GLM disagrees on the strict Trinitarian reading because Genesis 19:1 identifies two of the three as angels ("there came two angels to Sodom"), leaving one as the Lord (likely a pre-incarnation Christophany). The icon's *theological gesture* (hospitality as the form love takes among the persons of God) reaches for something true. Use a Protoevangelium-style "name and pivot" structure for Rublev:

1. Acknowledge what the iconography is doing (Trinitarian reading)
2. Identify where GLM's reading differs (Genesis 19:1 — two are angels)
3. Find the genuine theological gold the icon reaches for (hospitality as divine character; the pre-incarnation Christ at Mamre as "the angel of the LORD" Christophany tradition)
4. Affirm what's true; gently distinguish from what's overreach

Budget extra reflection length for Rublev — that one entry both anchors Coll 6 and models the contested-typology approach.

## Five-Mode Typology Taxonomy (locked in batch 008 review)

Coll 6 batch 008 demonstrated that "OT typology" is not one thing but a graded set of cases. The five modes below are the working framework. Reference by mode number when handling future contested-typology entries (Marian-Ark, Mary-as-Burning-Bush, Mary-as-Gideon's-Fleece, Mary-as-Jacob's-Ladder all go Mode 4; Christ-as-Lamb-of-God in OT lamb references would go Mode 3 or 5; etc.).

| Mode | Name | Canonical Example | What the Reflection Does |
|------|------|-------------------|-------------------------|
| **Mode 1** | Contested typology, partially corrected | #38 Rublev's Hospitality of Abraham | Name disagreement → identify gold → affirm what's true |
| **Mode 2** | One reading affirmed, another rejected | #39 Burning Bush at Sinai | Affirm Christophany (Acts 7:30); reject Marian-bush |
| **Mode 3** | Clean NT affirmation | #40 Sacrifice of Isaac at San Vitale | Heb 11:19 *en parabolē*; reflection confident |
| **Mode 4** | Iconographic typology entirely rejected | #41 Three Hebrews in the Furnace | Reject Christ-as-fourth; pivot to Heb 11:34 faith-witness |
| **Mode 5** | Dominical attestation — strongest case | #42 Jonah Sarcophagus | Christ himself names the type (Matt 12:40); unqualified |

**Mode 4 critical guardrail (locked in batch 008 review):** Be careful that "rejecting an iconographic typology" doesn't accidentally read as rejecting the iconography itself or the Fathers' faith. The typology is rejected; the icon's witness to deliverance and faith is affirmed. Distinguish:
- *The artwork* — affirm its theological witness (faith, deliverance, the saint's witness)
- *The typology* — reject when not NT-affirmed
- *The Patristic developers* — name them with respect; engage their reading charitably; disagree on text-based grounds

The Three Hebrews entry (#41) threaded this needle correctly: Origen, Ambrose, Hippolytus named with respect; KJV translation engaged charitably; disagreement framed as text-based (Aramaic *bar-elahin* + Daniel 3:28's "his angel" self-correction); pivot to Heb 11:34 affirms the icon's actual theological payload (faith and deliverance under fire).

## Collection 7 Framing Rule (locked pre-batch 009)

**Position:** Saints are biblical examples of faith, not objects of veneration. Hebrews 11 — the great cloud of witnesses chapter — is the canonical model. The chapter celebrates faith of OT figures *as examples to imitate*, not as intercessors to petition. This is the GLM frame for all saint iconography.

**Three live questions every Coll 7 entry will surface:**

- **Veneration** — the Catholic/Orthodox *latria* / *dulia* distinction (worship reserved for God; veneration permitted for saints). GLM rejects this distinction as practically untenable: in lived devotion, the line collapses. Saint imagery is engaged for what it teaches about Christian faithfulness, not as a focus of devotional practice.
- **Intercession** — whether saints intercede for the living. GLM holds they do not. Christ is the one mediator (1 Tim 2:5, already used at #27 Hodegetria and elsewhere). The communion of saints is a fellowship of faith, not a hierarchy of intercessors.
- **Apostolic continuity** — whether the apostles and martyrs are foundational figures *for our faith* (yes — Eph 2:20, the church built on the foundation of apostles and prophets) without being objects *of our devotion* (no — devotion goes to Christ alone).

**Specific case-types to handle differently:**

- **Apostles (Peter, Paul, John, etc.)** — easiest. Foundational, biblically attested. Treat them like Hebrews 11 treats Abraham — name their faith and the work they did, point at Christ.
- **Mary** — already calibrated in Collection 2. Cross-link rather than duplicate. Coll 7 entries that include Mary should defer to Coll 2 framing.

  **Marian cross-collection rule (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #73):** when a non-Coll-2 entry surfaces Marian iconography (in Coll 5 Life of Christ scenes, Coll 9 liturgical scenes, Coll 7 saints contexts, etc.), apply the same named-decline rule from Coll 2: *affirm what the iconography depicts* (Mary present, Mary praying, Mary directing) — *decline the mediatorial extrapolation* (Mary as mediatrix of grace, intercessor between humans and Christ). The Marian rule travels with the iconography wherever it appears.

  **Reference template:** #73 Wedding at Cana, Tokalı Kilise — Mary at Christ's right names the need (*they have no wine*) and tells the servants *whatsoever he saith unto you, do it* (John 2:5). The reflection keeps the order right: *Mary intercedes; Christ acts; Mary is not mediator of the miracle.* Affirm what the fresco depicts; decline the mediatorial extrapolation.

  **Especially important for Coll 9** — Marian iconography appears in many liturgical scenes (Pentecost, Dormition near eucharistic apse compositions, Communion-of-the-Apostles compositions, eucharistic-apse Theotokos with Christ-child). The cross-collection rule prevents drift in those settings.

  **Helvidian standing-rule lock (after #81 review):** for any future James / brothers-of-the-Lord entry, hold the **Helvidian reading** as the corpus's settled position — Mary and Joseph had children after Jesus. Anchored in **Matt 13:55–56, Mark 6:3, Matt 1:25, 1 Cor 15:7, John 7:5**. *No drift back toward Hieronymian (cousins) or Epiphanian (sons of Joseph by a prior marriage) framing in later entries* — both readings were built on top of the developing Marian-perpetual-virginity dogma the corpus does not affirm. Reference template: #81 James the Just, Pammakaristos.

  **Acts 15:28 imperative-phrasing lock (after #81 review):** *"it seemed good to the Holy Ghost, and to us"* is the textual ground severing *episkopos* from sacerdotal-chain transmission and grounding it in gathered deliberation under the Spirit. Whenever Acts 15 surfaces again in the corpus — second-James iconography, Coll 9 ecclesiology entries, any anti-apostolic-succession context — keep the imperative phrasing intact. Do not paraphrase the closing sentence away.
- **Early martyrs** (Stephen, Polycarp, Perpetua) — Stephen is biblical (Acts 7); Polycarp and Perpetua are historical witnesses with reliable martyrdom accounts. Affirm their faith; decline iconographic veneration.
- **Later canonized saints** (Nicholas, Catherine, George, Demetrios) — hardest standard case. Many have legendary accretions the corpus cannot affirm. **Pattern:** identify the historically defensible core; affirm the faith displayed; decline legendary embellishments and veneration. This is structurally analogous to the Coll 6 Mode 4 work — affirm the artwork's witness to faith, decline the legendary or devotional overreach.
- **Living-tradition saints in Eastern iconography** (Seraphim of Sarov, etc.) — most challenging because the iconography is recent and explicitly devotional. Mode 4-style move: affirm the historical figure, decline the iconographic devotional claim.

**Hebrews 11 as recurring anchor.** "Wherefore seeing we also are compassed about with so great a cloud of witnesses, let us lay aside every weight, and the sin which doth so easily beset us, and let us run with patience the race that is set before us" (Heb 12:1). The witnesses *witness*; they don't intercede. They are *examples*; they aren't *mediators*. The cloud surrounds the runner, but the runner runs to Christ.

**Closing imperative pattern for Coll 7 reflections:** redirect attention from the saint to Christ. The saint witnessed; we follow Christ. The saint died well; we live for Christ. The saint kept the faith; we run the race they finished.

### The "Named-Decline" Rule (locked in batch 009 review)

**When a saint has a documented intercession-cult, name the cult and decline it explicitly. When the saint doesn't have such a cult (Stephen, the canonical apostles), the framing rule operates silently through Hebrews 11 anchoring.**

**Reference templates:**
- **#43 Sergius and Bacchus:** *"We do not pray to Sergius and Bacchus. We do not light candles to them as intercessors. The mediation work in heaven and on earth belongs to Christ alone (1 Timothy 2:5)."*
- **#44 Demetrios:** *"We do not pray to Demetrios. We do not look to him for protection from Slavic raids or anything else."*
- **#46 Forty Martyrs:** *"We do not pray to the Forty. We do not light candles to them as intercessors."*

**When NOT to deploy:**
- Biblical saints whose veneration is light (e.g., #45 Stephen — the framing rule works implicitly through *"Go and do likewise"*)
- Mode-4 entries where legend-rejection already does the corrective work (e.g., #47 Nicholas — declining the legend absorbed the corrective frame; explicit cultic-rejection would have been redundant)
- Apostles in their canonical roles (Coll 4) — apostolic foundation is biblically affirmed; veneration-rejection would feel out of place

**Critical guardrail — the redirect must be Christological:**
Whenever the named-decline appears, the redirect target matters more than the rejection itself. The reader should leave the entry **looking at Christ, not at the absence of the saint.** Reference templates:
- #43 → Christ-medallion-between-them composition (the icon's own architecture redirects)
- #44 → John 18:9 (Christ has lost none given to him; protection belongs to Christ)
- #46 → Aglaius the converted pagan guard (the witness-converts-the-watcher evangelistic-pivot)

Future named-decline entries should pick a Christ-as-actual-object-of-trust redirect, not just a "saint isn't the answer" negation.

**Same-ladder formulation (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #72):** when declining a sacramental claim, locate the human figure being declined (priest, spiritual father, mediating saint) on the *same ladder* as the believer rather than on a categorically separate ladder above. The figure may be further up, may have real spiritual maturity, may be a genuine gift to the believer — but the ladder is the same, and Christ at the top is the destination for both. This preserves the dignity of the figure being declined while preserving Christ as sole mediator.

**Reference template:** #72 Ladder of Divine Ascent — *"The spiritual father is a fellow climber on the ladder, perhaps further up, but on the same ladder. Christ at the top reaches out his hand. The forgiveness is from him."*

This is the model close for every disputed-sacrament and named-decline entry going forward. When the corpus declines a sacramental claim, the redirect locates the believer's actual relationship — to God through Christ — without diminishing the human gift the tradition was honoring. The pastoral generosity of the formulation is the doctrinal point: the tradition's genuine gifts are not erased by the decline of the sacramental apparatus built on top of them.

**Horizontal-address sub-pattern (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #75):** the New Testament's standard form of address among believers and ministers is *horizontal* — *adelphos / adelphe* (brother / sister) is the apostolic vocabulary. The medieval ecclesiastical tradition introduced *vertical* address structures (father, son, master, novice, *padre*, *holy father*) that the apostolic literature does not supply for inter-believer relationships. When iconography or hagiography preserves the horizontal address, the corpus names it as itself doctrinal. **The horizontal address is the doctrine of the priesthood of all believers in linguistic form.**

**Reference template:** #75 Cappella Palatina Ananias and Paul — *"\"Brother Saul,\" he says — not \"son\" or \"catechumen\" or \"novice.\" Brother. The horizontal address is the doctrine."* Ananias addresses Saul as *adelphe* (Acts 9:17, the Greek vocative); the Palermo mosaicists preserved the horizontal posture.

This pairs with the same-ladder formulation: same-ladder is the *position* (figures on the same vertical axis); horizontal-address is the *language* (terms of address that match the position). Apply both whenever ordination, monastic-formation, or pastoral-mediation iconography surfaces.

**Canonical-collaboration sub-pattern (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #79):** biblical iconography that depicts canonical authorship as *collaborative* (apostle + scribe, prophet + recorder, gospel writer + named co-laborer) preserves what the New Testament itself attests: scripture descends through collaborative ministry, not through isolated revelation.

**Reference template:** #79 Xoranasat Gospel John-and-Prochorus — *"John does not write Revelation alone. The canonical text descends through two believers working together (apostle + disciple, in proportionate roles). Even canonical scripture is collaborative ministry; the iconography refuses the picture of the lone inspired writer."*

**Scriptural attestation across the canon:**
- Romans 16:22 — *"I Tertius, who wrote this epistle, salute you in the Lord"*
- Luke 1:1–4 — gospel composition explicitly described as research-based, drawing on multiple eyewitness sources
- Pauline epistles routinely co-authored or co-sent (Sosthenes at 1 Cor 1:1; Timothy at 2 Cor 1:1, Phil 1:1, Col 1:1, Philemon 1:1; Silvanus and Timothy at 1 Thess 1:1, 2 Thess 1:1)
- Mark's gospel descends through Mark from Peter's preaching (early patristic tradition)
- Hebrew Scriptures preserved through scribes, copyists, and the Masoretic tradition

**Apply at every entry depicting authorship, prophet-with-scribe, or evangelist-with-co-laborer scenes.** The corpus reads collaborative-authorship iconography as itself doctrinally meaningful — refusing both the lone-genius romantic picture and the priestly-mediation picture in the same move.

---

**John 15:15 *philous* extension (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #76):** Christ's own self-articulation in John 15:15 is the strongest scriptural anchor for the horizontal-address pattern. *"Henceforth I call you not servants; for the servant knoweth not what his lord doeth: but I have called you friends; for all things that I have heard of my Father I have made known unto you."* The Lord *refuses* the master-servant register that would justify hierarchical mediation among believers; he chooses *philous* — friends.

**The apostolic register is two-toned:** *adelphe* (brother / sister) for inter-believer address (#75 Ananias to Saul); *philous* (friends) for Christ's self-articulation toward the disciples (#76 foot-washing context). Together these form the New Testament's vocabulary for the church. Any iconographic or doctrinal pattern that reintroduces master-servant hierarchy among believers — *pater* and *filius* in the medieval ecclesiastical register — inverts what Christ himself established.

**Apply at every entry where ordination, monastic formation, or pastoral mediation surfaces.** When the iconography preserves the horizontal register, name it as itself doctrinal. When the iconography or its tradition introduces the vertical register, identify the departure from John 15:15 / Acts 9:17.

## Patristic Citation Rule (locked in batch 007 review)

Patristic sources appear in reflection bodies sparingly, when they do **decisive doctrinal-historical work that scripture alone cannot settle**. Specifically: when the question is *"is this iconographic detail rooted in early apostolic tradition or in later legendary embellishment?"*

Patristic sources witness to **history**, not to **theological authority**. Scripture remains the authority. Justin Martyr at #36 (dating the cave-Bethlehem tradition to c. 150 AD) is the right use — he is doing historical work, not theological work.

### Three-Tier Patristic List (locked in batch 009 review)

The Patristic list is structured in tiers governing **who** can be cited. The historical-work-only rule still applies across all tiers — the tiers govern citation scope, not citation purpose.

**Tier 1 — Earliest and most reliable (1st–3rd c.)** — broadly safe; cite freely for historical-witness work:
- **Clement of Rome** (c. 35–99) — apostolic-era; *1 Clement* is among the earliest non-canonical Christian writings
- **Ignatius of Antioch** (c. 35–108) — martyred under Trajan; his letters witness to the early church
- **Polycarp of Smyrna** (c. 69–155) — disciple of John, martyred c. 155
- **Justin Martyr** (c. 100–165) — earliest substantial Christian apologist
- **Irenaeus of Lyon** (c. 130–202) — apostolic-succession framework, anti-Gnostic
- **Tertullian** (c. 155–220) — Latin Father, safe on most points

**Tier 2 — Late patristic, doctrinally aligned (3rd–5th c.)** — cite for historical witness; doctrinally aligned with GLM on Trinity, Christology, incarnation:
- **Eusebius of Caesarea** (c. 260–340) — *Ecclesiastical History*, foundational historian
- **Athanasius of Alexandria** (c. 296–373) — *On the Incarnation*; defender of Nicaea
- **Cyril of Jerusalem** (c. 313–386) — *Catechetical Lectures*
- **Basil of Caesarea** (c. 330–379) — *On the Holy Spirit*; one of the Cappadocians
- **Gregory of Nazianzus** (c. 329–390) — Cappadocian, theologian-poet
- **Gregory of Nyssa** (c. 335–395) — Cappadocian, *Catechetical Oration*
- **John Chrysostom** (c. 347–407) — golden-tongued preacher; persecuted/exiled

**Tier 3 — Cite cautiously, only on points where GLM aligns**:
- **Origen** (c. 184–253) — brilliant but allegorical excess and questioned doctrines (universal restoration, pre-existence of souls); cite only where his exegesis matches NT-affirmed typology
- **Augustine of Hippo** (354–430) — towering theologian, but amillennial eschatology incompatible with GLM post-trib; infant-baptism position incompatible with GLM credo-baptism. Cite carefully on incarnation, Trinity, original sin, the *Confessions* and *City of God*'s broad sweep — but NOT on eschatology or sacraments
- **Jerome** (c. 342–420) — Vulgate translator (huge contribution); contentious on Mariology (perpetual virginity), monasticism, asceticism — cite for textual/historical work, not for those topics

**Avoid as theological authorities:**
- Late Byzantine Fathers (John of Damascus, Theodore the Studite, Gregory Palamas) — their iconology, mariology, eucharistic realism, and hesychast theology diverge significantly from GLM. Can be cited as historical witnesses to *what the Byzantine tradition believed*, but not as theological authorities supporting GLM positions.

## The Species/Figure Split Pattern (locked in batch 010 review)

Named pattern for handling iconography that **correctly identifies a theological reality** but **assigns the wrong actor to it**. Originated at #48 Saint George.

**Reference template (#48 George):** *"the iconography got the species right (the dragon is real) and the figure wrong."* The dragon is genuinely a New Testament adversary (Revelation 12); George is genuinely a faith-witness (testimony-army of Rev 12:11); but the dragon-slaying belongs to the Lamb, not to George. Affirm the reality. Correct the actor. Redirect to the canonical fulfiller of the right category.

**The pattern in three steps:**
1. Identify what the iconography *gets right* — the underlying theological category (dragon-as-adversary; lamb-as-sacrifice; fire-as-judgment; water-as-cleansing; etc.)
2. Identify what the iconography *gets wrong* — the specific figure-attribution (George as dragon-slayer; saint X as the bearer of theological reality Y)
3. Redirect to the canonical fulfiller — the actual New Testament agent of the theological category

**Applies beyond Coll 7.** Any iconography where the symbolism is sound but the human figure is misplaced.

**Distinct from Mode 4 typology rejection.** Mode 4 declines the typology entirely (Marian-bush, Christ-as-fourth-man). Species/figure split *affirms the underlying category* and corrects only the actor. Mode 4 says "this isn't a type." Species/figure split says "this IS a type, but you've identified the wrong fulfiller."

### Meta-pattern: Mode 4 + Species/Figure Split combination (locked in batch 012 review)

When an iconographic figure is **misassigned a Christological function**, both Mode 4 and species/figure split apply simultaneously. The icon got the figure's importance right; it gave them the wrong job. Two reference templates:

- **#62 Michael Weighing Souls** — Michael IS a real eschatological figure (Daniel 12, Jude 9, Rev 12) but he is NOT the judge of individual souls; that role is Christ's alone (John 5:22, 5:27; Acts 10:42; Rom 14:10). Affirm Michael's actual eschatological role; decline the Christological role assigned to him.
- **#48 Saint George** — George IS a real faith-witness (Diocletianic-era martyr, faithful soldier) but he is NOT the dragon-slayer; that role is the Lamb's (Rev 12:7–11). Affirm George's actual martyrological role; decline the Christological role assigned to him.

**The combination is the right move when:** (1) the iconography assigns a clearly Christological function (judging, dragon-slaying, sacrifice-receiving, redeeming) to (2) a real figure who has a *different* legitimate role in scripture. The reflection affirms the figure's actual role, declines the Christological misassignment, and redirects to the canonical Christ-as-fulfiller.

**Don't apply when** the figure has no scripture-warranted role at all — that's a simple Mode 4 rejection (Marian-bush typology, Christopher's giant-carrying-Christ legend, etc.). The combination requires *both* a real figure AND a misassigned Christological function.

## The Consolidation Rule (locked in batch 010 review)

**When iconographic tradition treats figures as a unit AND theological case-type is identical, consolidate into a single corpus entry. When iconographic tradition treats figures separately OR theological case-type differs, keep them as separate entries.**

**Reference template (#50 Three Hierarchs):** Basil, Chrysostom, and Gregory of Nazianzus consolidated into one entry. Iconographic tradition treats them as a unit (the 1100 AD feast was instituted to honor them together; they appear as a triad in standard Byzantine iconography). Theological case-type is identical (Tier 2 Patristic, Cappadocian theological-saints). Consolidation appropriate.

**Counter-example (Coll 4 anticipated):** Peter and Paul are typically depicted together iconographically (the *Concordia Apostolorum* pairing tradition is ancient) BUT their theological case-types differ:
- Iconographic attributes differ — Peter with keys, Paul with sword and book
- Theological roles differ — apostle to the Jews vs. apostle to the Gentiles (Galatians 2:7–8)
- Case-types differ — denial-and-restoration vs. conversion-and-mission

**Two separate entries warranted** despite frequent iconographic pairing. The consolidation rule requires *both* unity criteria (iconographic unity AND case-type identity).

**Applies to:** future warrior-saint pairings, evangelist clusters, prophet groupings, Marian iconographic types. Each pairing tested by both criteria.

The Eastern tradition names seven archangels; GLM affirms only the two biblical names.

### Coll 8 Named-Decline Rule (parallel to Coll 7)

- **When iconography depicts an angel cult or named non-canonical archangels with explicit veneration** → state GLM's position openly with text-based reasoning (Col 2:18, Rev 22:8–9).
- **When iconography depicts angels in their ministering role** (Annunciation, Resurrection, Christ-served-in-the-wilderness, Mamre theophany, Gideon's call, etc.) → framing rule operates silently. The angel is doing what scripture says angels do; no corrective is needed.

### "Ministering Not Mediating" — the Coll 8 Christological-redirect rule

**Angels minister *for* God to humans; they do not mediate *between* God and humans.** Christ alone mediates (1 Tim 2:5). When iconography surfaces an "angelic mediation" claim, the redirect goes Christological — same guardrail as the Coll 7 named-decline rule. Angels carry messages, fight wars, attend to saints — they do not stand between humans and God.

The phrase "ministering not mediating" is a one-line shorthand for the rule. Use it when the case-type forces the question.

### Named-Archangels Canonicity Rule

Only **Michael** and **Gabriel** are canonically attested archangel names. The corpus affirms both. Other named figures in Eastern Orthodox angelic-naming tradition (Raphael, Uriel, Sealtiel/Selaphiel, Jegudiel, Barachiel — the seven-archangels list) are deuterocanonical or extra-canonical and are **not affirmed by name in the corpus.** When iconography depicts named non-canonical archangels, apply the Mode 4 pattern: affirm the broader category (angelic ministry is real and biblical), decline the specific named figure as deuterocanonical, redirect to Michael/Gabriel as the canonical pair.

### Coll 8 Case-Types to Expect (directional, not prescriptive)

- **Annunciation Gabriel** — ministering, framing-rule silent. Already partially covered (#26 Chora Annunciation, #29 Sinai Annunciation).
- **Michael as warrior** — affirm; Daniel 10/12, Jude 9, Revelation 12 are sufficient warrant.
- **Three angels at Mamre** — already addressed at #38 Rublev (cross-link rather than duplicate).
- **Cherubim and Seraphim** — affirm Ezekiel 1, Isaiah 6, Revelation 4 as canonical; the iconographic four-faced/six-winged forms are biblical.
- **Named non-canonical archangels (Raphael, Uriel, etc.)** — Mode 4 work; affirm angelic ministry, decline the named figure as deuterocanonical, redirect to Michael/Gabriel.
- **Guardian angels** — nuanced. Matthew 18:10 attests *"in heaven their angels do always behold the face of my Father which is in heaven."* GLM affirms guardian angels as scriptural; declines the medieval personalization (named guardian, daily prayers to one's guardian).

**Closing imperative pattern for Coll 8 reflections:** when iconography shows an angel doing its biblical work, the close redirects from the angel to *the message the angel carries* or *the One the angel attends*. Angels do not collect attention; they pass it through to God.

## Site-Ceiling Soft Rule (locked in batch 007, explicated in batch 012 review)

Pastor Charlie locked the site-ceiling at **4 corpus entries per site** at batch 007 review (Daphni and Hosios Loukas were the originating cases). The rule is **soft, not hard.** Exceptions are allowed when *all three* of the following criteria are met:

1. **Iconic-of-site:** the iconography is genuinely iconic of that specific site, with no comparable substitute available elsewhere
2. **No substitute:** comparable iconography does not exist at a different site that would serve the corpus's theological coverage equally well
3. **Coverage-weakening:** the substitution would leave a major theological territory under-covered in the corpus

**When in doubt, hold the ceiling.** Substitution to a less-saturated site is usually the right move — it broadens the corpus's geographic and iconographic spread. The exception is for the rare case where the iconic-and-irreplaceable threshold is clearly met.

### Tracking conventions (locked in batch 014 mid-batch audit, after #83)

The state log was materially incomplete and partly inflated when audited at the close of batch 014. Conventions reset and the full inventory rebuilt below.

- **Architectural sites** are tracked by their physical-site identity (the building / monastery / church). Standard ceiling: 4 entries. Exception ceiling: 5, with all three site-ceiling exception criteria met *and* the exception flagged in the entry that triggers it.
- **Museum-housed icons** are tracked by **current housing**, not by original site, with a parenthetical noting the origin tradition where known but contested. The corpus is a tour of where Byzantine art can be found and seen.
- **Manuscripts** are a **separate category** with their own ceiling logic: a single illuminated manuscript may yield up to **3 entries** before approaching ceiling; **4+ requires an explicit exception call.** Manuscripts are not architectural sites and do not produce site-saturation in the same way a basilica does.
- **Tradition-type entries** (the Hodegetria type, where the original is lost and the iconography is a reproducible compositional vocabulary) are tracked separately with a single-entry ceiling — there is no specific physical artifact to add additional entries against.
- **Workshop / school entries** (panel icons attributed to a workshop or school rather than a specific site) are tracked under the workshop name with a parenthetical noting the icon's specific provenance is to be flagged when known.

## Coll 10 Framing Rule — Iconoclasm & the Iconography Debate (pre-locked for batch 013)

**The corpus rejects iconoclasm as overreach.** The Second Commandment forbids the worship of images, not their existence — Exodus 20:4–5 reads as a single prohibition, not two ("thou shalt not make... thou shalt not bow down to them and worship them"). God himself commanded images of cherubim above the ark (Exodus 25:18–22) and a bronze serpent in the wilderness (Numbers 21:8–9). The problem is never the image; the problem is the worship.

**The corpus also rejects Nicaea II's resolution as overreach in the opposite direction.** The veneration of icons (*proskynesis*) is not biblically distinguishable from the worship of icons (*latria*) in any sustainable practical way. The bronze serpent that God commanded became Nehushtan when Israel began to burn incense to it (2 Kings 18:4), and Hezekiah broke it. *Images that God himself commanded can become idols when veneration accumulates.*

**GLM holds a third position:** religious art as didactic, devotional, and witnessing — but never as a vehicle for veneration or petition. Icons can teach the gospel, can move the heart toward worship of God, can preserve the witness of saints and events, can serve as historical artifacts of the faith. **Icons cannot be venerated, kissed, processed in liturgy as objects of devotion, or addressed in prayer.** The line is not between image and no-image. The line is between teaching tool and devotional object.

### Scriptural anchors

- **Exodus 20:4–5** — single prohibition, properly read: *"Thou shalt not make unto thee any graven image... Thou shalt not bow down thyself to them, nor serve them."* The two clauses are connected; the prohibition is on image-worship, not image-existence.
- **Exodus 25:18–22** — God commands cherubim above the ark. Images that God himself commanded.
- **Numbers 21:8–9** — God commands the bronze serpent for healing in the wilderness.
- **2 Kings 18:4** — Hezekiah destroys the same bronze serpent because Israel had begun burning incense to it. Calls it *Nehushtan* ("a piece of brass"). **The Nehushtan trajectory is the corpus's sharpest scriptural argument against icon veneration.** A God-commanded image became an idol when veneration accumulated, and a faithful king destroyed it. Center Coll 10 on this.
- **John 4:24** — *"God is a Spirit: and they that worship him must worship him in spirit and in truth."* The worship-axis is spiritual-and-true, not material-and-iconic.

### Iconoclasts themselves — Mode 4 application

The iconoclastic emperors (Leo III, Constantine V) and the iconodule defenders (John of Damascus, Theodore the Studite) **both made theological errors.** The corpus does not lionize either side. The 8th-century controversy was a controversy in part *because both positions overreached.*

- **Iconoclasts overreached** by banning all religious imagery and destroying historic Christian art (a tradition the corpus depends on for its existence). Apply Mode 4: affirm the underlying concern (idolatry is real and dangerous) → decline the overreach (banning all religious imagery) → redirect to the third-position framing.
- **Iconodules overreached** by establishing the *latria* / *dulia* distinction and institutionalizing icon veneration as required orthodox practice. Apply Mode 4: affirm the underlying concern (incarnation makes visual representation theologically possible) → decline the overreach (mandatory veneration as orthodox practice) → redirect to the third-position framing.

### Coll 10 case-types to expect (directional, not prescriptive)

- **Sinai pre-iconoclasm survivors** (Pantocrator #2, Virgin Enthroned #24, Annunciation #29, Sergius/Bacchus #43, Saint Peter #56): already cross-tagged. Affirm survival; affirm the iconography's witness to incarnation; decline the cult that has accumulated.
- **Iconoclastic cross at Hagia Eirene** (BRIEF Coll 10 #2, c. 740s post-earthquake): rare iconoclastic-period artifact; affirm the iconoclasts' anti-idolatry concern; decline the overreach.
- **Khludov Psalter iconoclastic illuminations** (BRIEF Coll 10 #5, post-843): post-iconoclasm polemical illuminations attacking the iconoclasts. Mode 4 work — affirm the iconodules' incarnational concern; decline the iconoclast-as-Crucifier visual polemic; decline the overreach toward mandatory veneration.
- **Triumph of Orthodoxy icon** (BRIEF Coll 10 #3, 14th c.): commemorates 843 restoration of icons. Apply the both-sides-overreached corollary — the "Triumph" was real but did not authorize the cult that grew from it.
- **Council of Nicaea II depiction** (BRIEF Coll 10 #9): historically attest the council's role; theologically engage its overreach toward mandatory veneration.

### Coll 10 closing imperative pattern

**The Nehushtan question.** When the iconography surfaces an icon-veneration tradition, the closing redirect should ask the Hezekiah question: *what would Hezekiah do with this image?* If the image teaches Christ and the gospel, it stays — like the cherubim above the ark stayed. If the image has accumulated veneration to the point that worshippers are "burning incense to it," it goes — like Nehushtan went. The corpus's third position is not iconoclast (smashing all images) but Hezekian (smashing the ones that have become objects of cult).

### Coll 10 standing rule — read iconodule defenders at the moment they actually defended (locked at #110+2)

Any future iconodule-defender entry (John of Damascus when surfaced beyond #104 Tzanes, Patriarch Nikephoros, Theodore the Studite alongside the existing #110, Stephen the Younger alongside the existing #110, Methodios I, etc.) reads the defender at the iconographic moment they actually defended — **not as proxy support for later doctrinal positions they did not authorize**. Theodore's defense was incarnation-grounded (image-bears-reference logic, John 1:14 / Col 1:15 / Heb 1:3); the late-medieval mediation drift went further than his argument. Apply this principle as the standing iconodule-defender reading: affirm what the defender defended on apostolic ground; decline the later expansion the defender did not author. **Locked from #110.** Three-fence pattern: *affirm* defender's incarnation-grounded argument; *decline* the medieval-mediation expansion built on the defender's name; *refuse* the iconoclast counter-overreach that erases iconography altogether.

This is the most theologically loaded calibration remaining in the corpus. Take the time the work requires.

## Coll 4 Evangelist-Symbol Rule (pre-locked for batch 011)

The four evangelist-symbols — Matthew/man, Mark/lion, Luke/ox, John/eagle — derive from Ezekiel 1 (the four living creatures with four faces) and Revelation 4 (the four creatures around the throne). Irenaeus articulated the typological assignment in *Against Heresies* III.11.8 (c. 180 AD), making this **Tier 1 Patristic territory**. The symbols are well-attested in Christian tradition by the late 2nd century and become iconographically standard in Byzantine art.

**The corpus affirms the four-symbol typology as Tier 1 Patristic.** Acceptable iconographic tradition.

**Critical reading rule: each symbol is read Christologically rather than as primary identification of the evangelist.** The evangelist-symbol points to *Christ* through *the gospel that bears the evangelist's name*.

| Evangelist | Symbol | Christological Reading |
|------------|--------|------------------------|
| Matthew | Man (winged man) | Christ as the **Son of Man** whose human genealogy Matthew's gospel traces (Matt 1:1–17) |
| Mark | Lion | Christ as the **Lion of Judah** (Rev 5:5) whose royal authority Mark's gospel proclaims |
| Luke | Ox | Christ as the **sacrificial offering** Luke's gospel highlights (the temple-sacrifice motif throughout) |
| John | Eagle | Christ as the **high-soaring Logos** John's gospel ascends to (John 1:1) |

**Don't make the evangelist a symbol of himself.** Mark is not a lion; Christ is the Lion of Judah, and Mark's gospel proclaims Him. The symbol is the gospel's distinctive Christological note made visible.

**Applies whenever evangelist-symbols appear in Coll 4 iconography.** Often appear together as a tetramorph (Sant'Apollinare in Classe arch, Pammakaristos dome, etc.), often individually in evangelist-portrait pages of illuminated gospels (Rabbula Gospels, etc.).

Directional, not prescriptive. Land it however the specific iconography requires. The pre-stated rule prevents drift into decorative-symbolism readings.

## Coll 9 Liturgical — Pre-Batch Framing Rule (locked in batch 013 review, before batch 014 opens)

Coll 9 inherits two prior framing rules and adds one. The three rules together give Coll 9 the same tripartite framing infrastructure that Coll 10 had.

### Rule 1 — Memorial-view eucharistic anchor (transferred from Pillar 2)

The bread and cup proclaim Christ's death until he comes (1 Cor 11:26). The Spirit, not the elements, mediates Christ's presence. Apply at all eucharistic, communion, Heavenly Liturgy, and Mystic Supper iconography.

**Reference templates:** #30 Hosios Loukas apse Theotokos (memorial-view articulation); #71 Sant'Apollinare Nuovo Last Supper.

**Memorial-view-as-original-not-recovered (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #71 — 10th flagship doctrinal articulation):** the memorial-view position is **original to the institution narrative** (1 Cor 11:23–26), not a Reformation recovery. The scholastic eucharistic apparatus (transubstantiation, sacrifice of the Mass, priestly consecration) is what was built on top of the institution; the memorial reading is what the institution itself supplies.

The standard Protestant defense treats memorial-view as a 16th-century recovery — *Zwingli rediscovered what the church had drifted from.* That framing concedes too much. The institution sentence — *eis tēn emēn anamnēsin* (\"into my remembrance\") — is the memorial position. Transubstantiation is the innovation that has to argue backward against the institution text.

**Apply whenever the eucharistic apparatus is on the table.** Memorial-view is the original position. Other readings are downstream.

### Rule 2 — Ministering-not-mediating angels (transferred from Coll 8)

Heavenly Liturgy scenes show angels concelebrating with Christ; they do not mediate between Christ and the faithful. The angels are workers in the throne-room economy, not intercessors between humans and God.

**Apply at:** Heavenly Liturgy iconography, Communion of the Apostles where angels are present, any liturgical scene with angelic figures.

**Anchor pair:** Hebrews 1:14; Colossians 2:18 / Revelation 22:8–9.

### Rule 3 — Ordinance-not-sacrament (NEW — Coll 9 specific)

> Liturgical iconography depicts what humans do in worship; it does not depict what God does in response. The former can be honored as the church's witness to its own practice across centuries. The latter — the iconographic claim that God acts through the priest's hand or that grace is conferred through the sacramental action — is declined. The actor is always God, working through means he chooses; the priest is a minister, not a mediator.
>
> The seven-sacrament tradition is declined. **Baptism and the Lord's Supper are received as ordinances commanded by Christ.** The other five (confirmation/chrismation, confession, marriage, anointing, ordination) are honored where they reflect biblical patterns (James 5:14, 5:16; Ephesians 5; the laying-on of hands) but declined where they claim sacramental status beyond what scripture supports.

**Anchors:**
- 1 Corinthians 11:23–26 (memorial / Lord's Supper as ordinance)
- Matthew 28:19 (baptism commanded as ordinance)
- James 5:14–16 (anointing and confession as biblical practice, not sacramental conferral)
- Ephesians 5:32 (marriage as Christ-church symbol, not sacrament)
- 1 Timothy 2:5 (one mediator, the man Christ Jesus — anti-priestly-mediation)
- 1 Peter 2:9; Revelation 1:6 (priesthood of all believers)

**Sub-anchor for baptism entries (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #70):** **1 Peter 3:21** is the primary scriptural anchor for any Coll 9 baptism-related entry, alongside Matthew 28:19 for the command itself. *"The like figure whereunto even baptism doth also now save us, (not the putting away of the filth of the flesh, but the answer of a good conscience toward God,) by the resurrection of Jesus Christ."* The verse acknowledges that baptism saves, specifies that the salvation is *not* through physical water-cleansing, and locates the saving power in the resurrection received by good conscience. The cleanest possible refutation of baptismal regeneration from inside the regenerationist's strongest proof-text. Future Coll 9 baptism-related entries should pattern-match to this verse first.

**Built-on-top-of distinction (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #71):** for every disputed-sacrament case-type in Coll 9, the iconography depicts a *scriptural practice*; the *sacramental claim* was built on top of the scene rather than carried by it. Affirm what the iconography shows; decline what was added. This is the structural rule for every disputed-sacrament entry in Coll 9.

| Case-type | What the iconography depicts | What was built on top |
|-----------|------------------------------|------------------------|
| Lord's Supper | A meal — Christ giving, disciples receiving (Matt 26 / 1 Cor 11) | Transubstantiation, sacrifice of the Mass, priestly consecration |
| Baptism | A water-figure of union with Christ's death and resurrection (Rom 6, 1 Pet 3:21) | Baptismal regeneration ex opere operato |
| Confession | Mutual confession — *"one to another"* (Jas 5:16) | Auricular confession to a priest as grace-conferring sacrament |
| Anointing | Elders praying over the sick (Jas 5:14) | Last rites as grace-conferring sacrament |
| Marriage | A public covenant; a Christ-and-church symbol (Eph 5:32) | Sacramental matrimony as grace-conferring |
| Ordination | Laying-on of hands (1 Tim 4:14; 2 Tim 1:6) | Apostolic-succession-by-touch; sacerdotal-grace transmission |
| Confirmation/chrismation | Reception of the Spirit at conversion (Acts 8, 19) | Chrismation as grace-conferring second-stage sacrament |

In every case: iconography is faithful to scripture; sacramental claim was added later. **Affirm; decline; redirect to scripture.**

**Translation-becomes-doctrine pattern (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #73):** when a Vulgate translation choice supplies a Latin term that carries theological weight the Greek original doesn't sustain, the corpus distinguishes the Greek meaning from the Latin elaboration. Affirm what the Greek says; identify where the Latin theological apparatus expanded the term beyond its scriptural sense; decline what was built on the translation rather than on the text.

**Tracked Vulgate translation–doctrine pairs:**

## Layered-Site Rule (locked in batch 014 opening, before first Coll 9 entry)

When a single iconographic site contains multiple distinct theological subjects, the corpus may write multiple entries (one per subject), each cross-tagged appropriately. The primary entry should focus on a single theological subject; supporting iconography gets a brief mention but does not pull the reflection off-task.

**Examples already operating:**
- **Sant'Apollinare in Classe** — Cosmic Cross + Apostolic Flock as separate entries
- **Daphni** — Pantocrator + Crucifixion + Annunciation + Presentation as separate entries
- **Hosios Loukas** — separate entries for apse Theotokos, Pentecost dome, narthex Christ
- **Santi Cosma e Damiano** (this batch) — apex Lamb as Coll 9 entry; Christ-in-glory and saint-presentation composition reserved for a possible later Coll 7 entry

**Why this matters:** layered sites tempt the writer toward over-comprehensive entries that try to cover everything in one reflection. The result is reflections that drift across multiple theological subjects without landing any of them clearly. Single-subject entries land the doctrinal payload; supporting iconography gets cross-tag references.

**How to apply:** when opening an entry on a layered site, identify which single iconographic element is the theological subject of *this* entry. Mention the surrounding composition briefly (one sentence is usually enough). Do not let the supporting iconography pull the reflection into a second or third theological subject.

**Iconographic-discipline observation (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review):** the layered-site rule isn't a modern editorial preference — it tracks the iconographic discipline of the artists themselves at their best. When iconography strips its supporting cast (e.g., the Sant'Apollinare Nuovo loaves-and-fishes mosaic at #69, which reduces the gospel scene of thousands to Christ + two disciples + bread + fish), the corpus follows the iconography's lead. When iconography is layered, the corpus writes single-subject entries that mirror the iconographer's focal choices.

This grounds the rule in the iconographic tradition itself, not just the corpus's editorial preference. Future Mode-1 and Mode-2 reflection work should ask first: *what did the iconographer focus on?* — and follow that focal choice.

**Iconographic-survival principle (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #70):** iconography that stays close to scripture survives the controversies its framers couldn't have foreseen. When an entry surfaces iconography that has passed through doctrinal transitions intact (Arian → orthodox at #70 Arian Baptistery; iconoclast → iconodule at the Sinai pre-iconoclasm survivors #2 / #20 / #24 / #66; pre-Reformation programs preserved through Catholic / Orthodox / Reformed eras), the survival itself is theologically meaningful and worth naming.

The pattern: scripturally-disciplined iconography is what survives. The Arian Baptistery's mosaic survived its building's transfer from Arian to orthodox use precisely because the iconographer depicted what the gospels say happened (Father's voice, Son in water, Spirit descending) without adding the heretical Arian gloss. The discipline preserved the work across the transition.

The principle pairs with the layered-site rule and the iconographic-discipline observation: the corpus's editorial discipline is the same discipline the best iconographers exercised at the moment of making.

**Palimpsest-as-iconographic-survival sub-pattern (locked at #110+2, anchored at #95 Saint Nicholas Boyana):** when an entry surfaces iconography that has been *literally* layered — multiple paint layers or restoration phases visible on the same surface, with the underlying figure preserved across the layering — read the palimpsest itself as a literal demonstration of the iconographic-survival principle. What stays across the layers (face, posture, identifying attributes) versus what changes (vestment-vocabulary, surface details registering cultural-liturgical period) is the visual proof that iconography close to its scriptural-historical core survives the cultural revisions imposed on its surface. **Deploy sparingly** — at most one or two more uses across the remaining corpus before the framing becomes a tic. Reserve for entries where multiple paint layers actually survive in viewable form, not metaphorically-layered iconography.

**Compositional theology (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #72):** the iconographer's compositional choices — eye-lines, gaze direction, framing, scale, position — are themselves theological work. Where the composition directs the viewer's eye is itself a doctrinal claim about where attention belongs. The corpus reads composition as doctrine, not just depiction.

**Reference template:** #72 Ladder of Divine Ascent — *"Look at the icon again. Notice who the climbers are looking at. Not at Climacus at the foot of the ladder. Not at the angels around Christ. At Christ at the top. The composition's eye-line is doctrine."*

**Asymmetric-attention sub-pattern (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #74):** when iconography depicts *asymmetric* attention — Christ seeing the figure who cannot yet see, Christ reaching toward the figure who has not yet reached, Christ initiating contact with the figure who is unable — the asymmetry itself is doctrine. **Grace is initiated by God, not earned by the seeker.** The composition tracks the doctrine; the corpus names what it tracks.

**Reference template:** #74 Rossano Gospels Healing of the Blind Man — *"Every figure in the scene whose eyes can see is looking at Christ. The blind man cannot yet — but Christ is looking at him."* The compositional asymmetry encodes a doctrine of grace. The blind do not see Christ until Christ sees them first.

The sub-pattern adds a soteriological dimension to the directional eye-line observation: not just *where* the attention is sent, but *who initiates* the attention. Apply whenever iconography depicts an asymmetric encounter (healings, Christ calling Lazarus from the tomb, Christ reaching to Peter on the water, Christ at Emmaus before recognition, etc.).

**Sequential-attention sub-pattern (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #79):** when iconography stages a *chain of attention* across multiple figures — each looking at a different target rather than all looking at one — the *sequence itself* is doctrinally meaningful.

**Reference template:** #79 Xoranasat Gospel John-and-Prochorus — heavenly arc → John looking up → Prochorus looking down at the scroll. The doctrine of how scripture comes to be is rendered in a single composition: God speaks; apostle hears and speaks; disciple writes; church receives. *John is the conduit; he is not the source.*

Distinct from the two prior compositional-theology observations:
- **Eye-line-as-doctrine** (#72 Climacus) — *unitary*: all figures look at Christ
- **Asymmetric-attention** (#74 Rossano blind man) — *binary*: Christ initiates; figure receives
- **Sequential-attention** (#79 Xoranasat) — *staged chain*: multiple figures, multiple targets, doctrinal sequence

The three sub-patterns under compositional theology give a precise vocabulary for reading composition as theological argument. Apply whichever fits the iconography. When multiple apply (e.g., a Patmos scene with both sequential attention to the scroll and asymmetric attention to the heavenly source), name both.

**Cross-tradition convergence sub-observation (locked in batch 014 mid-batch review, after #77):** iconography produced in non-Reformation traditions sometimes embodies Reformation-aligned doctrine in compositional form *before* the doctrine is verbally articulated. The compositional theology and the verbal theology converge because both are anchored in the same scriptural text.

**Reference template:** #77 Georgian Pentecost enamel — apostles in a curved bench *circling* rather than ranking; no senior apostle elevated; the arc is a circle of equals rendering the priesthood-of-all-believers in iconographic form four centuries before the Reformation. The 12th-century Georgian iconographer was not reading Reformation tracts; he was reading Acts 2 and rendering what scripture says (*all with one accord*) into compositional form (*a circle of equals*).

**Why this matters for DU's argument:** the corpus is not claiming Byzantine iconography was secretly Protestant. The corpus is claiming that *scripturally-disciplined iconography in any tradition converges on the same doctrine because it is reading the same text.* That is a scholarly claim, not a polemical one — and it is what makes DU's iconographic argument credible across confessional boundaries.

Apply whenever iconography in a non-Reformation tradition (Byzantine, Coptic, Ethiopian, Georgian, Armenian, Syriac, etc.) embodies in compositional form a doctrine the Reformation later articulated verbally. Name the convergence; trace it back to the shared scriptural source; refuse anachronism.

## Opener-vs-Establisher Rule (locked in batch 014 mid-batch correction)

Collection openers *demonstrate* the framing rules in action; they do not *establish* the framing rules from scratch. Establishment work happens in voice-templates.md before the batch opens. Openers may run toward the collection's soft ceiling but should not exceed it.

If an opener requires more length than the ceiling allows, the cause is usually that the framing wasn't fully locked in voice-templates.md before the batch opened — fix the framing, not the entry length.

**Why this matters:** if the opener runs over the ceiling, the next entry justifies itself relative to the opener rather than the original calibration. Word counts drift across a collection without anyone deciding to allow it. Catch the drift in the opener, not after five entries.

**Inversion check:** the Closing Note below says reflections should be written from the artwork itself; the templates are calibration anchors, not formulas. If extra words are spent re-articulating the framing rules in their full doctrinal exposition, the framing rules are pulling the reflection away from the artwork. The framing should land the iconography; when the framing becomes the subject, the iconography becomes the pretext.

**Applies retroactively to every future collection opener** (Coll 4 expansion entries, any new collection territory).

### Polemical register-restraint — standing rule (formalized at #142 after three demonstrations)

**Standing rule:** the corpus does not amplify iconographic vitriol against historical opponents even when the iconography itself does. Read what the iconography argues; decline what the iconography spits.

**Provenance:** surfaced at #115 Nikephoros Khludov Psalter; a second demonstration at #133 Madrid Skylitzes argument-about-icons (rendering the iconoclasts as *arguing*, not as demonized); a third demonstration at #142 Leo V Madrid Skylitzes (emperor argues; monk witnesses; both treated with apostolic-restraint register). Three consistent demonstrations across three Coll 10 entries cross-manuscript-tradition. Pattern is now formalized as standing rule.

**Why this matters:** much of the corpus's territory is in collections (Coll 7, Coll 10, sometimes Coll 4) where iconography itself carries polemical charge against named historical opponents (iconoclasts, heretics, the *other side* of doctrinal disputes). The corpus's discipline reads the doctrinal argument carried by the iconography while declining to amplify the polemical vitriol the iconography may also carry. The standing rule applies retroactively to all polemical iconography in the corpus and prospectively to all future polemical entries. Apostolic-line precedent: 1 Cor 8:7 (*every man hath not that knowledge*); Rom 14:1–13; 2 Tim 2:24–26 (*the servant of the Lord must not strive; but be gentle... in meekness instructing those that oppose themselves*).

**Operational application:** apply the restraint where polemical iconography surfaces; do not force-apply to iconography that isn't polemical. The pattern is reactive (declining amplification) not prescriptive (mandating an iconography stay irenic). The corpus reads what the iconographer argues and witnesses to what scripture says; the corpus does not pile-on the polemical-vitriol register the iconographer may have invested in his composition.
