## 1. Identification

- **Object:** The Anastasis (Greek ἀνάστασις, "resurrection" / "raising up") fresco occupying the conch (semi-dome) of the apse of the parekklesion at the Chora Church. The composition shows Christ in the *moment between* Good Friday and Easter Sunday — the descent into Hades. Christ in white robes, in a blue-and-white starred mandorla, stands on the broken gates of Hades (the gates form a cross under his feet); he simultaneously pulls Adam by the right wrist (raised from his tomb on the right) and Eve by the left wrist (from her tomb on the left). The Old Testament righteous stand behind Adam and Eve: King David, King Solomon, John the Baptist, Abel, and others, identified by Greek inscriptions and royal regalia.
- **Dynamic composition.** The Chora Anastasis is widely cited as the most psychologically and kinetically charged of all surviving Anastasis frescoes. Christ is in motion — robes flying, body torquing left-and-right, simultaneously pulling both first parents out. The composition registers the resurrection as *active conquest*, not passive emergence.
- **Medium:** Fresco. The parekklesion's entire program is fresco (not mosaic), unlike the main Chora narthex/naos which mixes mosaic and fresco.
- **Date:** c. 1315–1321. Theodore Metochites's restoration program, same window as the narthex Pantocrator (#9 in batch 002). The parekklesion's full eschatological cycle (Last Judgment, Anastasis, Second Coming attendant scenes) was painted in this campaign as a coordinated funerary program — the parekklesion housed multiple tombs of Metochites's family and associates.
- **Location:** Chora Church / Kariye Camii, Edirnekapı, Istanbul. Same site as #9 in batch 002. Approx. 41.0312° N, 28.9394° E.
- **Era:** Late Byzantine.
- **Period:** Palaeologan Renaissance.

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- **Anastasis as Eastern Resurrection iconography.** The Western artistic tradition shows the Resurrection as Christ stepping out of the tomb alone (the type that becomes standard in Italy from Giotto onward). The Eastern Anastasis shows Christ descending into Hades and dragging the dead out. Kartsonis (1986) traces the divergence to the 8th–9th century. The Eastern type is theologically richer because it stages the *corporate* resurrection — Christ does not just rise; he raises others.
- **Wrist-grabbing iconography.** Adam and Eve are gripped at the wrist, not the hand. This is universal in the Byzantine Anastasis tradition. The theological point: salvation is monergistic — the agency is Christ's, not Adam's. Adam does not reach up; Christ reaches down. Adam does not have to grab on; Christ grabs him.
- **OT righteous as audience.** The kings and prophets behind Adam and Eve are identified figures (David, Solomon, John the Baptist, Abel) who lived before Christ's incarnation. Their presence in the scene visualizes the doctrine that OT believers were saved by Christ's death and resurrection retroactively (1 Pet 3:19; Eph 4:8). The Byzantine reading of OT salvation is forward-looking: the righteous of Israel waited in Sheol/Hades for Christ's descent.
- **Funerary program.** The parekklesion was a tomb chapel. Multiple tombs were located in the chapel and around it. The Anastasis was painted to be looked at *by mourners standing over a recently buried family member.* The pastoral function is direct: death is not the end because Christ has already conquered it.
