## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Mosaic of the Annunciation at the Well (Annunciation at the Spring) on a pendentive of the inner narthex of the Chora Church. Mary stands beside a public spring, drawing water with a clay pitcher; she turns over her shoulder in surprise. The Archangel Gabriel descends from the upper right, gesturing toward her. The composition is twisted to fit the triangular pendentive surface — the diagonal axis between angel and Virgin runs across the pendentive's length, and Mary's body torques to fit the lower point.
- **Iconographic source:** The well/spring setting is from the *Protoevangelium of James* (a 2nd-century apocryphal infancy gospel, also called the *Book of James*), which places a first Annunciation at the public well of Nazareth before the canonical Lukan Annunciation in Mary's house. The Byzantine artistic tradition typically depicts both moments — first the Annunciation at the Well (the scene at this entry), then the Annunciation at the loom or in the house (Luke 1:26–38). Both scenes appear in the Chora's mosaic program.
- **Date:** c. 1315–1321. Theodore Metochites's restoration program. Same window as the Chora Pantocrator (#9 in corpus) and the Chora Anastasis (#17). The third Chora entry in the corpus.
- **Medium:** Wall mosaic on gold ground.
- **Location:** Chora Church / Kariye Camii, inner narthex, on a pendentive of the northern dome. Approx. 41.0312° N, 28.9394° E.
- **Era:** Late Byzantine.
- **Period:** Palaeologan Renaissance.

The Chora's Mary cycle in the inner narthex is one of the most extensive surviving in Byzantine art. It includes: Joachim's expulsion from the temple, Joachim's prayer in the wilderness, Anne's prayer in the garden, the Annunciation to Saint Anne, Joachim and Anne's embrace, Mary's birth, Mary's first seven steps, Mary blessed by the priests, Mary in the Temple, Mary fed by an angel, Mary's instruction, the giving of staffs to the suitors, Joseph receiving the rod, the betrothal, Joseph leaving Mary, **the Annunciation at the Well**, Joseph reproaching Mary, the journey to Bethlehem, the Annunciation to Joseph, the Nativity, the Adoration of the Magi, etc. Most of this cycle is from the Protoevangelium tradition, not the canonical Gospels.

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- **The Protoevangelium of James.** The "Annunciation at the Well" scene is not in the canonical New Testament. It is from the *Protoevangelium*, a 2nd-century Greek apocryphal gospel that elaborates Mary's biography (her birth to Joachim and Anne, her childhood in the temple, her betrothal to Joseph, the well annunciation, etc.). The Byzantine tradition received this text widely and used it as iconographic source material. Pastor Charlie's audience is Protestant; the Protoevangelium is not in the Protestant canon and was not received as scripture by the Reformers. The reflection acknowledges this honestly: "We do not need the apocryphal narrative to read what the artist is teaching."
- **Two-stage Byzantine Annunciation.** The Byzantine iconographic tradition typically depicts BOTH a first Annunciation at the well (Protoevangelium) AND a second Annunciation in the house with Mary's consent (Luke 1:26–38). These are usually paired in adjacent mosaics. The Chora has both. The pendentive mosaic at this entry is the well scene; another pendentive nearby shows the house scene.
- **Composition forced by architecture.** The pendentive's triangular shape forced the artist into a diagonal/twisted composition. Underwood (1966) and others read this not as a constraint accident but as a feature: the awkwardness of Mary's turn registers the surprise of being interrupted. The composition itself stages "interruption."
- **Mary's consent.** Luke 1:38's "be it unto me according to thy word" (Greek *genoito moi kata to rhēma sou*) is the canonical theological pivot. The reflection makes this the focal scripture, even though the artwork shows the Protoevangelium scene that precedes it.

## 6. Open questions

- **Engagement with apocryphal source.** I named the Protoevangelium directly. This is honest scholarship and consistent with BRIEF.md §2's "we engage with the *theological content* of icons... rather than veneration practices" — engaging the doctrinal content even when the iconographic source is non-canonical. If you want a more direct critique of the Protoevangelium (e.g., "we do not accept this as scripture"), I can sharpen. My read: the line "we do not need the apocryphal narrative to read what the artist is teaching" carries the position implicitly. A flat critique would distract from the reflection's incarnational pivot.
- **"Exemplary woman" critique.** The reflection ends with "do not preach Mary as exemplary woman." Some Protestant teaching does emphasize Mary as a model of faith ("be it unto me" as obedience example). This is not necessarily Marian devotion; it can be sound Protestant exegesis (Mary's faith is real, her obedience is real, Luke records it as exemplary). The reflection's "do not preach Mary as exemplary woman" might be too sharp if read in isolation. Context: the reflection is correcting against making Mary's exemplarity the *primary* teaching point; the *primary* teaching point should be the incarnation. Mary's faith/obedience is real but secondary to what her faith was IN. If you want this softened ("do not preach Mary *only* as exemplary woman"), easy edit.
- **Mary's body language.** I described the composition as "twisted" and "awkward" and Mary's posture as "surprised." Underwood reads it as registering "the surprise of being interrupted." The reflection runs with this. If you'd prefer a less psychological reading, I can dial back to compositional description.
- **The two Annunciations.** Byzantine iconographic tradition has both an apocryphal well-Annunciation and a canonical house-Annunciation. The Chora has both as separate mosaics. This entry covers the well scene; the house scene at the Chora is a separate mosaic that could be a future entry. Pairing the two would be redundant (same theological doctrine); we use the well scene because it's the more iconic image and because the doctrinal pivot in the reflection (Luke 1:35–38) is the same regardless of which scene is depicted.
