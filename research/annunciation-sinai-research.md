## 1. Identification

- **Object:** A late-12th-century portable icon of the Annunciation, currently held at Saint Catherine's Monastery, Mount Sinai. The composition shows Mary seated frontally on a backless throne in a stylized rocky landscape, with a small river at her feet; the Archangel Gabriel descends from the upper right against a starry sky. Mary holds a length of red/scarlet fabric in her left hand. Gabriel's facial expression is intense and notably troubled.
- **Iconographic source for the veil-weaving detail:** The *Protoevangelium of James* (chapter 10) states that Mary had been selected by lot to weave the scarlet/purple thread for the temple veil in Jerusalem, and that the Annunciation interrupted this work. This non-canonical narrative is the source for many Byzantine Annunciation iconographic details, including the red yarn / temple veil cloth in Mary's hand.
- **Iconographic distinctiveness.** The throne-in-landscape composition is unusual — most Byzantine Annunciations stage the scene indoors (Mary standing or seated in a domestic interior, sometimes with a loom or a chair). The Sinai 12th-c. Annunciation places her outside on a stylized landscape throne; scholars (Evans/Wixom) have suggested this may indicate the icon was produced at Sinai itself rather than in Constantinople, drawing on local iconographic traditions.
- **Medium:** Tempera on wooden panel, with gold ground.
- **Dimensions:** 61 × 42 cm.
- **Date:** Late 12th century. Late Komnenian.
- **Location:** Saint Catherine's Monastery, Sinai. Same site as Sinai Pantocrator (#2), Sinai apse (#14), Sinai Virgin and Child (#24). The fourth Sinai entry in the corpus.
- **Era:** Middle Byzantine.
- **Period:** Late Komnenian (Komnenian dynasty 1081–1185; this icon may date to the Angelos dynasty 1185–1204 if late-12th century is read past 1185).

## 3. Sources consulted

- Smarthistory, ["Art and architecture of Saint Catherine's Monastery at Mount Sinai"](https://smarthistory.org/saint-catherines-monastery-sinai/).
- *Protoevangelium of James*, chapter 10, accessible in J.K. Elliott, *The Apocryphal New Testament* (Oxford, 1993) — for the veil-weaving narrative.

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- **The Protoevangelium veil narrative.** Protoevangelium of James 10:1–2 places Mary among seven virgins of the tribe of David selected by lot to weave the temple veil; she draws the lot for the scarlet/purple. The veil-weaving is interrupted at Gabriel's annunciation in chapter 11. This is the iconographic source for the red yarn detail in many Byzantine Annunciation icons (including this Sinai example, the Cefalù Annunciation in Sicily, and others).
- **Hebrews 10:20 connection.** The reflection's pivot is Hebrews 10:20's claim that Christ's flesh is "the veil" through which we have access to the holiest. This makes the Protoevangelium veil-detail iconographically and theologically lined-up: Mary weaving the scarlet veil at the moment of conceiving Christ-the-veil. The reflection makes this connection explicit and treats it as the icon's accidental theological brilliance — the apocryphal narrative is non-canonical, but the iconographic detail (when paired with Hebrews 10) becomes a powerful visual theology of the veil-as-flesh.
- **Gabriel's troubled face.** Multiple scholars (Evans/Wixom 1997 in the Met catalog; Maria Vassilaki 2000) note Gabriel's expression is intensely focused and arguably grieving — atypical for Annunciation iconography which usually shows Gabriel in joyful posture. The reflection makes use of this: the joy of the announcement is shadowed by foreknowledge of the cross.

## 4. Scripture verification (KJV)

**Hebrews 10:19–20:**
> 19 Having therefore, brethren, boldness to enter into the holiest by the blood of Jesus,
> 20 By a new and living way, which he hath consecrated for us, through the veil, that is to say, his flesh.

Reflection quotes vv. 19–20 with v. 20's "his flesh" emphasized. **Verified.** This is one of the most significant single phrases in Hebrews — calling Christ's flesh "the veil" (Greek τοῦ καταπετάσματος, *tou katapetasmatos*, "the curtain"; the same word used for the temple veil in LXX Exodus 26:31–33 and in Matthew 27:51).

**Matthew 27:51:** "And, behold, the veil of the temple was rent in twain from the top to the bottom; and the earth did quake, and the rocks rent." **Verified.**

**Luke 1:26–38** (in `scripture_refs`): the canonical Annunciation passage. Verified at #26 Chora Annunciation earlier.

**Exodus 26:31–33** (in `scripture_refs`, not quoted): "And thou shalt make a vail of blue, and purple, and scarlet, and fine twined linen of cunning work... And the vail shall divide unto you between the holy place and the most holy." Foundation OT text for the temple veil; **verified.**
