## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Mosaic of the Crucifixion in the north arm of the catholicon at Daphni Monastery. The composition is austere and triangular: Christ on the cross at the apex; Mary at the foot of the cross on the right (Christ's right side, viewer's left); John the Apostle on the left (Christ's left side, viewer's right). Gold ground. No additional figures (no thieves, soldiers, or onlookers).
- **Iconographic details:**
  - **Christ's body** — naked except for a loincloth, head bowed slightly toward Mary, eyes closed, body sagging on the cross
  - **The wound** — the spear-wound in Christ's right side, with blood and water flowing in distinct streams (per John 19:34)
  - **Adam's skull** — small skull at the base of the cross, representing Adam (per the apocryphal/patristic tradition that Calvary was the burial site of Adam)
  - **The titulus** — the inscription "ΙC ΧC" / "ΒΑCΙΛΕΥC ΤΗC ΔΟΞΗC" (Jesus Christ / King of Glory) above the cross
- **Date:** c. 1090–1100. Same Komnenian-era mosaic program as the Daphni Pantocrator (#4 in our corpus), Daphni Presentation of the Theotokos (#32), and the broader Daphni cycle.
- **Medium:** Wall mosaic on gold ground.
- **Location:** Daphni Monastery, Chaidari, near Athens, Greece. UNESCO World Heritage Site (1990, with Hosios Loukas and Nea Moni). Same site as #4 and #32. Approx. 38.0136° N, 23.6358° E.
- **Era:** Middle Byzantine.
- **Period:** Middle Byzantine, Komnenian.

The Daphni Crucifixion is universally cited as one of the supreme achievements of Byzantine Crucifixion iconography — Cormack (2000) calls it "the most refined Byzantine treatment of the subject"; Mouriki (1980/81) places it alongside the Daphni Anastasis as the apex of the Daphni master's psychological-classicizing style.

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- **Blood and water from the side.** John 19:34 is the proof-text for the wound iconography. Patristic tradition reads the blood and water typologically: blood = the eucharist, water = baptism (so e.g. Augustine, Tract. on John 120). Byzantine mosaicists rendered the two streams visibly. The reflection treats this as anti-docetic (Christ really died and bled) without engaging the sacramental-typological reading (which would be Catholic/Orthodox-leaning); the GLM-memorial-view template established at #30 stays in the background here.
- **Adam's skull at the base.** The patristic tradition (already established by Origen and Tertullian) that Calvary was the site of Adam's grave is non-canonical but theologically rich. The reflection treats it as legend ("the old legend") and uses the iconographic detail to drive a 1 Cor 15:22 typology rather than to assert the legend's historicity. Same general move as Protoevangelium "name and pivot" — name the non-canonical source, treat it as non-canonical, pivot to canonical scripture (1 Cor 15:22).
- **John 19:26–27 ecclesiology.** Christ's instruction to Mary and John ("Woman, behold thy son!... Behold thy mother!") is canonically the founding moment of the post-Pentecost ecclesia in Catholic Marian theology. The reflection takes a more modest reading: Christ is making provision for his earthly mother and his closest disciple; the church-formation reading is implicit but Mary is treated as a member of the church, not as its mother in any salvific sense.

- **No engagement with the sacramental reading of blood-and-water.** Patristic tradition (Augustine, Chrysostom) reads the two streams sacramentally (water = baptism, blood = Eucharist). The reflection deliberately stays at the historical-physical level (real wound, real blood) per the GLM memorial-view template. If you want the sacramental reading developed (in a memorial-view-friendly way), easy add. My read: the reflection is already long (~395 words); adding sacramental typology would push past the word cap and dilute the anti-docetic core argument.

This is a measured Christological-not-Marian reading: Mary is a member of the founding gospel community, not the co-redemptrix at Calvary. If the framing reads as too soft on the Catholic Marian-co-redemption tradition, easy to sharpen.
