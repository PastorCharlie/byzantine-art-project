## 2. The martyrs' history

Forty soldiers of the *Legio XII Fulminata* ("Lightning") at Sebaste in Lesser Armenia (modern Sivas, Turkey) were ordered c. 320 AD by the prefect under Licinius (Licinius was the eastern co-emperor who briefly tolerated Christianity then resumed persecution before his defeat by Constantine in 324) to renounce Christ. They refused, were stripped naked, and exposed on a frozen pond overnight. A warm bath was placed on the shore as inducement.

According to Basil of Caesarea's *Homily on the Forty Martyrs* (preached c. 373, within fifty years of the event):
- One soldier broke and ran to the bath; he died as the warmth touched his frozen body.
- A pagan guard named Aglaius, watching, saw a vision of crowns descending from heaven onto the soldiers' heads. Realizing one crown was unclaimed, he stripped, joined them in the lake, and the count returned to forty.
- By morning all were dead. Their bodies were burned and the ashes thrown into a river; the Christians of Sebaste recovered remains where they could.

Basil's homily is corroborated by Gregory of Nyssa's homily on the same Forty Martyrs (also late 4th c.) and by Ephrem the Syrian's hymns. The martyrdom is therefore one of the best-documented early Christian martyrdom events.
