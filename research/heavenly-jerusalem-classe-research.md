## 1. Identification

- **Object:** The Heavenly Jerusalem composition on the triumphal arch above the central apse of the Basilica of Sant'Apollinare in Classe. The arch is a multi-register program; from top to bottom:
  1. **Christ in central medallion** — bearded, a more developed Pantocrator-type than the youthful Christ at Sant'Apollinare Nuovo (#13 in batch 002). Indicates that the arch's central Christ may be a 7th-century or later addition, after the bearded Pantocrator type stabilized.
  2. **Four Living Creatures** (Rev 4:6–8): the lion (Mark), ox (Luke), man (Matthew), and eagle (John), arranged around the central medallion.
  3. **The Twelve Apostles** as bust portraits in two flanking rows of six.
  4. **The Heavenly Jerusalem and Bethlehem** — paired walled cities, labeled with Latin inscriptions HIERVSALEM and BETHLEEM. From each city's gate, six sheep emerge and walk toward Christ in the medallion. Total of twelve sheep — the apostolic flock, parallel to the lambs in the apse below.
- **Apse below the arch (referenced but not the focal artwork):** Cosmic cross with stars, hand of God descending from clouds, Moses and Elijah at the sides (a Transfiguration composition), Saint Apollinaris standing as orant figure among twelve sheep.
- **Medium:** Wall mosaic, glass tesserae on gold ground.
- **Date:** Multi-phase. The lower apse (Apollinaris with sheep, the cross, Moses/Elijah) is 6th-century, dated to or shortly after the basilica's consecration in 549. The triumphal arch with the Heavenly Jerusalem and the central Christ medallion is generally dated 7th century, with later 12th-century additions. For the data record I used "6th–7th century (apse 549; arch 7th c.)" with sort range 549–700.
- **Location:** Basilica of Sant'Apollinare in Classe, in Classe (Classis), the ancient port suburb of Ravenna. UNESCO World Heritage Site (1996, "Early Christian Monuments of Ravenna" — same inscription as Sant'Apollinare Nuovo #13). Approx. 44.3792° N, 12.2300° E.
- **Era:** Early Byzantine.
- **Period:** Early Byzantine, spanning Justinianic (apse) and the early Exarchate of Ravenna (arch). Ravenna was the Byzantine exarchate capital from 584 to 751.

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- **Bethlehem-Jerusalem pairing.** This iconographic pairing (sheep emerging from both walled cities) is rare and theologically deliberate. The flock that constitutes the church comes from both events — incarnation and crucifixion-resurrection. The mosaicists are arguing pictorially that you cannot bisect the gospel. (Deichmann 1969, vol. 2.)
- **Latin labels.** HIERVSALEM and BETHLEEM are spelled in Latin, not Greek — visible distinction from the bilingual Norman-Sicilian programs (Cefalù, Monreale) where Greek is also used. Ravenna's late 6th–7th c. context was Latin-Christian under Byzantine political authority; the Greek-Latin bilingual culture was less prominent than in Sicily.
- **The arch as eschatological destination.** Reading the apse + arch together: the apse shows the present church (Apollinaris with his flock); the arch shows the eschatological destination (Christ in glory, the cities, the heavenly flock walking in). The basilica's program walks the worshipper visually from earthly church (lower) to heavenly church (upper) on the way to the altar. Von Simson (1948) develops this reading.

## 5. Reflection authorship

Fresh draft. Distinguishing angle: **the heavenly city as a real place, not a metaphor.** The reflection corrects a tendency in modern (especially Western Protestant) eschatology to treat "heaven" as a state or condition rather than a city with gates and walls. Revelation 21–22 is architectural, and the Byzantine artists treated it that way.

Tone choices:
- Direct opening as a doctrinal claim: "There is a city ahead, and it is real."
- The Latin labels (HIERVSALEM, BETHLEEM) quoted as concrete textual evidence — these are still visible 1,400 years later.
- The Bethlehem-Jerusalem pairing developed as a theological pivot: incarnation + crucifixion-resurrection are both required gates.
- "*Past tense.*" emphasized — the city is already built. The believer is walking toward something already prepared.
- Pastoral application as direct correction: "do not flatten it to 'going to heaven' in some abstract sense."
- Closing line: "The sheep at Classe were going somewhere specific. So are we." — direct, parallel construction, present tense.

## 6. Open questions

- **Apse and arch as one artwork or two.** The BRIEF lists "The Heavenly Jerusalem, Sant'Apollinare in Classe" (#14) and "Christ enthroned with archangels, Sant'Apollinare in Classe" (#17) as separate artworks. The same basilica is also referenced via the Four Living Creatures (#15) and the 24 Elders concept (#16). The BRIEF appears to be treating the same physical basilica as the source for several distinct iconographic features. This entry covers the Heavenly Jerusalem on the arch. A future entry could focus on the apse Christ-as-cross / Transfiguration program separately. The image I have shows both, which is fine for this entry but could double-feature in the apse entry too.
- **Bethlehem in the Coll 3 frame.** I included Bethlehem (incarnation) in a Second-Coming reflection. The pairing earns its place because the mosaic itself pairs the two cities, but a strict reader might say "Bethlehem belongs in Coll 2 or Coll 5, not Coll 3." My read: the artwork itself joins them, so the reflection joins them. Documenting in case you read it differently.
- **The Christ medallion on the arch is bearded.** I noted in §1 that this suggests a 7th-c. or later addition (the bearded Pantocrator type stabilized after c. 565). Some scholars (Deichmann) date the arch's central Christ to the 12th century. The reflection doesn't engage with this dating debate because it would distract from the heavenly-city focus, but the date range in the JSON (549–700) is broad enough to accommodate either reading.
