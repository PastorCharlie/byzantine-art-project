# Research Note — Michael Weighing Souls (Coptic Psychostasia Icon)

**Slug:** `michael-weighing-souls` | **Compiled:** 2026-04-29 (batch 012)

## Quick reference
- **Object:** 18th-c. Coptic icon of Michael holding the scale of judgment (psychostasia). Coptic Museum, Cairo. Michael in martial pose, gold ground.
- **Image:** PD-Art (Wikimedia, uploaded by Gabriel Philadelpho), 1530×2220, 1.5 MB.
- **Iconographic source:** Egyptian funerary religion (Thoth/Anubis weighing the heart against Maat's feather) → Byzantine and Eastern Christian Last Judgment iconography → Coptic standalone composition.

## Calibration — Mode 4 + species/figure split combined
- **Species/figure split applied** (per the locked-in pattern at #48 George): the *species* is right (there IS a weighing, a judgment, a verdict at the parousia) but the *figure* is wrong (the weigher is Christ, not Michael).
- **Scripture-warranted Michael:** Daniel 12:1 (defender of God's people in the time of trouble), Jude 9 (lawful adversary refusing to bring railing accusation against the devil), Revelation 12:7 (warring against the dragon). Three real eschatological roles, all affirmed.
- **Scripture-rejected: Michael as judge of individual souls.** John 5:22, John 5:27, Acts 10:42, Romans 14:10 all assign judgment to Christ alone. The Egyptian-Coptic iconographic borrowing assigned the weighing function to a familiar angel-with-scale figure (analogous to Anubis-with-scale before him), but the canonical text gives the function to the Son.
- **Coll 8 + Coll 3 cross-tag earned:** psychostasia is angel-iconography (Coll 8) and Last Judgment iconography (Coll 3). Both collections register this entry.
- **Closing imperative:** *"do not preach Michael with the scale. Preach Christ on the throne. The Son of Man, who lived a human life, is the Judge of human lives. Michael will be there. He will not be the one weighing."* — direct application of John 5:27 ("the Son of man").

## Coptic-tradition note
The reflection treats the Coptic origin of the icon transparently. Coptic Christianity is in communion with the Eastern Orthodox tradition broadly, with Christological-theological distinctions (Miaphysite vs. Chalcedonian) that don't bear on the psychostasia iconography itself. The reflection engages the iconography without entering Christological-controversy territory.
