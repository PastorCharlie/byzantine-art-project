## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Christ Pantocrator at the apex of the cupola (small dome) over the choir/sanctuary, surrounded by eight angels in postures of worship. Right hand raised in benediction; left hand holds the gospel codex. Below the dome drum: rows of evangelists and prophets descending the architectural hierarchy.
- **Note on the chapel's two Pantocrators:** The Capella Palatina contains TWO Christ Pantocrator mosaics — one in the cupola (the subject of this entry, c. 1143, Roger II's commission) and one in the central apse semi-dome (later addition, traditionally attributed to William II's program). The cupola Pantocrator is the canonical "Capella Palatina Pantocrator" referenced in BRIEF.md ("Palermo, 1140s") because the dome inscription dates the 1143 program.
- **Medium:** Wall mosaic, glass tesserae on gold ground.
- **Date:** c. 1143 — established by an inscription within the dome program. Cathedral construction 1132–1140; mosaic program completed in stages 1140–1170; the cupola is the earliest and highest-quality phase.
- **Location:** Capella Palatina (Palatine Chapel), within the Palazzo dei Normanni (formerly Royal Palace of the Norman kings of Sicily), Palermo, Sicily. UNESCO World Heritage Site (2015, with the Cefalù and Monreale cathedrals as part of "Arab-Norman Palermo"). Approx. 38.1141° N, 13.3528° E.
- **Era:** Middle Byzantine.
- **Period:** Norman Sicilian (Byzantine craftsmen under Roger II) — same period label as Cefalù #3, applied to a different commission by the same king.

## 3. Sources consulted

- William Tronzo, *The Cultures of His Kingdom: Roger II and the Cappella Palatina in Palermo* (Princeton, 1997) — the definitive monograph on Roger II's chapel as a programmatic statement of Norman Sicilian kingship.
- Otto Demus, *The Mosaics of Norman Sicily* (London, 1949).
- Eve Borsook, *Messages in Mosaic: The Royal Programmes of Norman Sicily 1130–1187* (Oxford, 1990).
- Wikipedia, ["Cappella Palatina"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cappella_Palatina).
- Smarthistory, ["The Cappella Palatina"](https://smarthistory.org/cappella-palatina/).

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- Roger II (r. 1130–1154) ruled a tricultural kingdom of Latin Christians, Greek-speaking Byzantines, and Arabic-speaking Muslims. The Capella Palatina embodies all three: Byzantine mosaic technique and iconography, Latin Romanesque architecture, and an Islamic muqarnas wooden ceiling painted by Fatimid craftsmen.
- The chapel was Roger's personal/court chapel, not a public church — built into the royal palace. Tronzo (1997) reads the entire program as a political theology: the king who hears mass under the Pantocrator is publicly acknowledging that he himself answers to Christ.
- The apse Pantocrator was a later addition to bring the chapel program into closer parallel with Roger II's cathedral at Cefalù (#3). For a "1140s" entry, the cupola is the right choice.
