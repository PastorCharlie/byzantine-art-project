# Research Note — Christ Pantocrator, Chora Church (Kariye)

**Slug:** `pantocrator-chora`
**Collection:** Pantocrator only — single collection. The "Land of the Living" reading has eschatological resonance but the reflection's center is Christ-as-place (a here-and-now identification), not eschatology proper. Earned-not-padded principle: no secondary.
**Compiled:** 2026-04-28

---

## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Christ Pantocrator in the lunette mosaic over the doorway from the outer narthex into the inner narthex. Right hand raised in benediction; left hand holds a jeweled gospel-book. The figure is monumental — the lunette is approximately 3.6 meters wide and 2.1 meters tall; the face alone is approximately 76 cm.
- **The inscription:** Greek majuscule, in a band above the figure, reads ΙC ΧC Η ΧΩΡΑ ΤΩΝ ΖΩΝΤΩΝ — "Jesus Christ, the *chora* of the living." The name *Chora* is itself a wordplay: the church was originally called "Saviour in Chora" because it stood outside the city walls (*chora* = countryside, hinterland), but Theodore Metochites's 14th-century restoration program redeployed the name as a Christological claim — Christ is the *chora*, the dwelling-place, of the living.
- **Medium:** Wall mosaic on gold ground, in the refined Palaeologan style.
- **Date:** c. 1315–1321. Theodore Metochites (Grand Logothete of emperor Andronicus II Palaeologus) financed the restoration of the church and its mosaic and fresco program in this window.
- **Location:** Church of the Holy Saviour in Chora (Greek: ἡ Ἐκκλησία τοῦ Ἁγίου Σωτῆρος ἐν τῇ Χώρᾳ; Turkish: Kariye Camii), Edirnekapı neighborhood, Fatih district, Istanbul. Approx. 41.0312° N, 28.9394° E. Currently functions as a mosque (re-converted 2020); the mosaics are publicly visible.
- **Era:** Late Byzantine.
- **Period:** Palaeologan Renaissance — same period as Pammakaristos #7. The Chora program is widely considered the apex of Palaeologan-era mosaic and fresco work and is contemporary with the Pammakaristos parekklesion.

## 3. Sources consulted

- Paul A. Underwood, *The Kariye Djami* (4 vols., Bollingen, 1966–1975) — the definitive scholarly publication of the Chora mosaics and frescoes; volume 1 covers the historical introduction including Metochites's program; volumes 2–3 are the mosaic plates.
- Robert Ousterhout, *The Art of the Kariye Camii* (London, 2002) — accessible monograph integrating Underwood with subsequent scholarship.
- Helen C. Evans (ed.), *Byzantium: Faith and Power (1261–1557)* (Met, 2004) — for Palaeologan context.
- Wikipedia, ["The Chora"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Chora).
- Smarthistory, ["Picturing salvation — Chora's brilliant Byzantine mosaics and frescoes"](https://smarthistory.org/picturing-salvation/).

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- Theodore Metochites (c. 1270–1332) was a court intellectual, philosopher, and statesman under Andronicus II. His restoration of the Chora was a personal piety project and one of the great Byzantine cultural undertakings of the 14th century. Metochites is depicted in another mosaic in the inner narthex offering a model of the church to the enthroned Christ.
- The "Chora" wordplay is well-established in Byzantine literature. Underwood (1966, vol. 1) and Ousterhout (2002) both develop it as the theological key to the entire program: the name appears in three inscriptions in the church, each redeploying it Christologically (Christ as Chora of the Living; Theotokos as Chora of the Uncontainable; etc.).

This is the third "Pantocrator over a doorway" reflection in the corpus (Hosios Loukas #5, Cefalù #3 with the codex-as-message, Chora #9). Each takes a different theological angle:

## 6. Open questions

- **No secondary collection.** The "land of the living" theme has eschatological resonance, but the reflection lands on a here-and-now identification (Christ as place) rather than on judgment or second coming. Following Pastor Charlie's "earned not padded" principle.
- **Multi-Pantocrator at Chora.** The Chora has additional Pantocrator imagery (a Deesis composition in the inner narthex, possibly destroyed dome programs in the naos). The "Land of the Living" lunette is the canonical and most-photographed Pantocrator at Chora and the natural choice for BRIEF.md's single-entry slot.
