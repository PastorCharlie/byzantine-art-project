## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Christ Pantocrator, half-length figure dominating the conch of the central apse. Right hand raised in benediction; left hand holds an open codex with bilingual Greek-Latin inscription (John 8:12 + 14:6 conflated, same pattern as Cefalù).
- **Scale:** This is the **largest surviving Pantocrator mosaic in the Byzantine corpus.** The figure occupies the full apse semi-dome; the face alone is approximately 2 meters across.
- **Medium:** Wall mosaic on gold ground.
- **Date:** c. 1180–1190. Cathedral construction began 1174 under William II of Sicily; the mosaic program is the largest surviving Byzantine-style cycle in the West (~6,500 m²) and was executed in the 1180s by Byzantine workshops imported from Constantinople. BRIEF.md gives "1180s"; my range matches.
- **Location:** Cathedral of Monreale (Duomo di Monreale, formal: Cattedrale di Santa Maria Nuova), Monreale, near Palermo, Sicily. UNESCO World Heritage Site (2015). Approx. 38.0814° N, 13.2925° E.
- **Era:** Middle Byzantine.
- **Period:** Norman Sicilian (Byzantine craftsmen under William II) — same cultural-fusion label as Cefalù #3, applied to a different king and a later (and grander) program.

## 3. Sources consulted

- Otto Demus, *The Mosaics of Norman Sicily* (London, 1949) — establishes Monreale's program as the apex of Norman-Sicilian Byzantine mosaic work.
- Eve Borsook, *Messages in Mosaic: The Royal Programmes of Norman Sicily 1130–1187* (Oxford, 1990).
- William Tronzo, "Byzantine Court Culture from the Point of View of Norman Sicily" in *Byzantine Court Culture from 829 to 1204* (Dumbarton Oaks, 1997).
- Wikipedia, ["Monreale Cathedral mosaics"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monreale_Cathedral_mosaics).

Key facts:
- William II (r. 1166–1189) commissioned Monreale at the height of Norman Sicilian power. Demus reads it as William's response to Roger II's Cefalù — bigger, more elaborate, more theologically programmatic.
- The whole cathedral interior is a unified mosaic theological program: Old Testament cycle in the nave, New Testament cycle in the transepts, Pantocrator + apostolic deesis in the apse. It is the most complete Byzantine-style mosaic cycle outside the East.
- The Pantocrator overflows the apse — the figure was scaled to be visible from the cathedral entrance, hundreds of feet away.
