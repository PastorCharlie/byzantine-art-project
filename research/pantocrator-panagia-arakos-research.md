# Research Note — Christ Pantocrator, Panagia tou Arakos (Lagoudera)

**Slug:** `pantocrator-panagia-arakos`
**Collection:** Pantocrator only — single collection. Reflection center is faithfulness/labor in obscurity, which doesn't pull cleanly into any other collection. Earned-not-padded.
**Compiled:** 2026-04-28

---

## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Christ Pantocrator in the central medallion of the dome, surrounded by archangels and prophets in the drum below. One of the canonical Komnenian-era Pantocrators in the surviving corpus.
- **Medium:** Fresco — painted directly onto the wet plaster. Second fresco-medium artwork in the Pantocrator batch (after Mistra #10).
- **Date:** **December 1192**, precisely dated. The Greek dedicatory inscription on the north wall lintel records both the year and the painter's name — this is one of very few precisely-dated and signed Byzantine fresco programs.
- **Painter:** Theodore Apsevdis (Θεόδωρος Ἀψευδής), a Constantinople-trained monk-painter. The rare named-artist case in this batch.
- **Patron:** Lord Leon (Leontios) Authentes, per the inscription.
- **Location:** Church of Panagia tou Arakos (Panagia Arakiotissa, "the Mother of God of the [Vetch/Pea]") — a small Byzantine church in the village of Lagoudera, in the Troodos Mountains of central Cyprus. Approx. 34.9544° N, 33.0064° E. The church is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (1985, as part of the "Painted Churches in the Troödos Region" inscription).
- **Era:** Middle Byzantine.
- **Period:** Late Comnenian. The Komnenian dynasty had ended in 1185; Cyprus had passed from Byzantine control to Crusader rule in 1191 (Richard the Lionheart's conquest, then sale to Guy de Lusignan). The 1192 date places this fresco at the very end of Byzantine Cyprus and the start of the Lusignan kingdom — Byzantine artistic tradition continuing under Latin political rule.

Key contextual facts that informed the reflection:
- **Named artist.** Theodore Apsevdis is one of the very few named Byzantine wall painters whose work survives. The inscription on the north lintel (still visible) names him explicitly: "δι' ἐξόδου τοῦ ἐντιμοτάτου κυρ Λέοντος τοῦ Αὐθέντη... διὰ χειρὸς Θεοδώρου τοῦ Ἀψευδοῦς, μηνὶ Δεκεμβρίῳ ἔτους ϚΨ′" — "at the expense of the most honorable Lord Leon Authentes... by the hand of Theodore Apsevdis, in the month of December, year 6701 [= AD 1192]."
- **Constantinople-to-village transit.** Apsevdis's training is identifiable as Constantinopolitan based on stylistic analysis (Winfield 2003). The reasons for his presence in a remote Troodos village are unknown but speculation includes (1) flight from political turmoil after the Komnenian collapse, (2) following his patron Leontios Authentes, who may have been a Byzantine refugee following the Latin takeover.
- **Political timing.** Cyprus passed from Byzantine to Crusader rule in 1191. Apsevdis painted in 1192. The fresco is therefore a Byzantine art program executed under Latin political rule — Byzantine Christianity persisting in the rural communities even as the political map changed. This is the same theological territory as Mistra #10 (faithfulness in collapse) but at a smaller, more local scale.

## 5. Reflection authorship

Fresh draft. Distinguishing angle: **named labor in obscurity.** This is the only artwork in the Pantocrator batch that turns on the *artist* rather than the *patron*, *architecture*, or *iconography*. Apsevdis is the rare named Byzantine painter; the small village location plus the clear date plus the political context (Latin takeover one year prior) make this a "Christ painted in obscurity by a man who can be named" piece.

The reflection lands on a pastoral imperative ("Do your work") that mirrors and extends 1 Cor 15:58 directly. No "Be Obedient. Be Bold." sign-off — the close "Do your work" is itself a strong imperative landing, and the sign-off was just used at #10.
