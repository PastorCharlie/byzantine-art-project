## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Christ enthroned amid four angels, occupying the terminus of the procession-of-male-martyrs cycle on the upper register of the south wall (interior nave) of the Basilica of Sant'Apollinare Nuovo. 26 martyrs, each carrying a golden crown, advance from Theodoric's palace (depicted at the west end of the wall) toward the enthroned Christ at the east end. A parallel program on the north wall shows female martyrs and virgins advancing toward the enthroned Theotokos.
- **Note on iconographic type:** This is **not** a standard Byzantine Pantocrator (no dome medallion; no bearded mature figure; no cross-nimbus halo with the standard ΙC ΧC monogram). It is instead an *early Christ-enthroned* image — youthful, beardless, in Roman imperial robes, holding a scroll. The bearded Pantocrator type that becomes canonical in Byzantine art (visible in Sinai #2, c. 550) had not yet stabilized when this mosaic was made. The Sant'Apollinare Nuovo image preserves what some scholars call the "early Constantinian" or "Apollo/Sol Invictus" image-type for Christ — solar, imperial, youthful. The BRIEF lists it under Pantocrator (Collection 1) because functionally it serves as an enthroned-sovereign image of Christ; iconographically it is pre-Pantocrator.
- **Medium:** Wall mosaic on gold ground. Glass and stone tesserae.
- **Date:** Early 6th century. The basilica was built c. 504 by Theodoric the Great (king of the Ostrogoths, ruler of Italy 493–526) as his palatine chapel and originally dedicated to "Christ the Redeemer." Theodoric was an Arian Christian, and the original mosaic program was designed in an Arian theological framework. After Justinian's reconquest of Italy (Belisarius's campaigns 535–540) and the dechristianization of Arianism, the basilica was reconsecrated as Catholic c. 561 under Bishop Agnellus of Ravenna. Parts of the original program were edited at this time — notably, the lower register of the procession was retouched to remove identifiable Theodoric-era figures. The Christ-enthroned panel itself is variously dated; recent scholarship leans toward Justinianic-era retouching of an originally Theodoric-era figure. For the data record I have used 504 as `date_year_start` (original construction) and 561 as `date_year_end` (Justinianic edit).
- **Location:** Basilica of Sant'Apollinare Nuovo, Ravenna, Italy. UNESCO World Heritage Site (1996, "Early Christian Monuments of Ravenna" inscription, with Sant'Apollinare in Classe and six other Ravenna monuments). Approx. 44.4172° N, 12.2034° E.
- **Era:** Early Byzantine.
- **Period:** Early Byzantine (Theodoric / Justinianic). This is the Western Byzantine outpost — Ravenna was the political seat of the Western Roman Empire's last decades and then of Theodoric's Ostrogothic kingdom and then of the Byzantine exarchate after Justinian's reconquest. The mosaic program is theologically and stylistically Byzantine but politically situated in the Latin West.

Key contextual facts that informed the reflection:
- **Theodoric → Justinian theological recoding.** The original Theodoric program was Arian — that is, it represented Christ in ways that could be read in subordinationist Arian theology (Christ as a creature, divine but not co-equal with the Father). After Justinian's reconquest the program was edited to remove what was overtly Arian. The Christ-enthroned panel as it survives today is the *Catholic* Christ — fully divine, enthroned with imperial dignity. This is the only artwork in the batch whose iconography was contested theologically across two regimes.
- **Revelation 4 staging.** Deichmann (1958, vol. 1) and von Simson (1948) both read the procession-toward-throne mosaic as a deliberate visual staging of Revelation 4–5: the elders/martyrs cast their crowns before the enthroned Lord. The basilica's nave is therefore designed as the ekphrasis of John's heavenly throne-room vision.
- **Image-type history.** The youthful beardless Christ-type was the dominant image of Christ in early Christian art (3rd–5th century catacombs and sarcophagi). The bearded mature Pantocrator type stabilized in the late 5th–6th century in the Eastern empire and gradually displaced the youthful type. Sant'Apollinare Nuovo preserves the older type at the moment when both types still circulated.
