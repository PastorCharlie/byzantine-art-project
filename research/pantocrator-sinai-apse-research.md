## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Apse mosaic of the Transfiguration, occupying the conch (semi-dome) of the apse of the Katholikon (main basilica) at Saint Catherine's Monastery. Christ is centered in a blue mandorla (oval glory), beardless and youthful in the early image-type, with seven rays of light emanating to the surrounding figures. To his right: Moses. To his left: Elijah. Below, three apostles in postures of awe and prostration: John (left), Peter (center, kneeling), James (right). All six of the surrounding figures are labeled with Greek inscriptions naming them.
- **Above the apse on the triumphal arch:** Two related Sinai scenes — Moses at the burning bush (left), Moses receiving the tablets of the law (right). The pairing makes the apse a typological argument: the law-giving on Sinai and the Transfiguration on Mount Tabor are the same revelation seen from two sides.
- **Note on iconographic type:** Like Sant'Apollinare Nuovo (#13), this is the *youthful beardless Christ* type, not the bearded Pantocrator type that would later become canonical. Sinai shows both image-types in one monastery: this 6th-century apse Christ is youthful; the slightly later 6th-century encaustic Pantocrator (#2) is bearded. Both were made within decades of each other in the same monastery's collection.
- **Medium:** Wall mosaic, glass and stone tesserae on a gold-and-blue ground.
- **Date:** c. 565. The basilica was built by Justinian I; conservation studies (Forsyth & Weitzmann 1973; Mango 1976) date the apse mosaic to the period between the death of Theodora (548) and the death of Justinian (565), based on a dedicatory inscription naming Longinus the abbot and reading "for the salvation of those who have ordered" the work — phrasing that suggests Justinian was still living. This is one of the most precisely-datable mosaics in the early Byzantine corpus.
- **Location:** Saint Catherine's Monastery, Sinai Peninsula, Egypt. Same site as Sinai Pantocrator #2; both artworks are from the same 6th-century imperial commission, separated by only decades. Approx. 28.5559° N, 33.9760° E.
- **Era:** Early Byzantine.
- **Period:** Justinianic.

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- **Sinai-on-Sinai.** The basilica is built at the foot of Mount Sinai (Jebel Musa), the traditional site of the Mosaic theophany. The apse program juxtaposes Sinai-Moses scenes with the Transfiguration. Forsyth & Weitzmann (1973) and Mango (1976) read this as a deliberate typological program — the Transfiguration is read as the Christological completion of the Sinai theophany. This is the strongest theological move available for a reflection on this artwork.
- **Image-type contrast with Sinai #2.** This mosaic uses the youthful beardless Christ-type; the encaustic icon (Sinai #2) uses the bearded Pantocrator-type. Both are 6th-century, both at Saint Catherine's. The image-type choice was apparently a stylistic decision per artwork, not yet a stable convention.
- **Survival via isolation.** Same as Sinai #2. The 8th-century iconoclasts could not reach Sinai. The Transfiguration apse is therefore one of only a handful of surviving major pre-iconoclasm apse programs from anywhere in the Byzantine world.

## 6. Open questions

- **Title.** I used "Christ in the Transfiguration" with subtitle "Apse Mosaic, Saint Catherine's Monastery." This is technically not a Pantocrator (the figure is a Transfiguration Christ in a mandorla, not an enthroned Pantocrator). The BRIEF lists it under Pantocrator (Collection 1 #14) as "Pantocrator from the Sinai apse," which functionally treats this Transfiguration Christ as Pantocrator-type. If you want the title to read "Christ Pantocrator" to match the BRIEF's collection naming, easy edit; I went with "Christ in the Transfiguration" because that's iconographically accurate and helps when this same artwork appears in Collection 5 (Life of Christ) where it's listed as "Transfiguration."
- **Cross-listing in BRIEF.** This is the first artwork in the batch that the BRIEF explicitly lists in TWO collections (#14 in Coll 1 and #6 in Coll 5). The data model handles this fine via `collection_primary` + `collection_secondary` + `collections` array. When we batch Collection 5 later, this entry already exists; we won't create a duplicate.
- **Image resolution.** Lowest-res master in the corpus so far at 2250 × 1527. CC0 cleanliness preferred over higher-res CC BY-SA alternatives that don't exist for this specific mosaic. If you want a sharper master, the path is the St. Catherine's Foundation archive in London.
- **Greek inscriptions on figures.** Each of the six surrounding figures (Moses, Elijah, Peter, James, John) carries a Greek label. I did not transcribe them in this research note because the labels are not theologically central — they're just identifications. If you want them documented, easy add.
