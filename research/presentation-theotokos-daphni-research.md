## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Mosaic of the Presentation of the Theotokos (the Eastern feast of *Eisodos tēs Theotokou*, "the Entry of the Theotokos into the Temple"), in a squinch of the catholicon at Daphni Monastery. Composition: a small Mary (depicted as approximately 3 years old per the Protoevangelium narrative) at the foot of the temple steps; her parents Joachim and Anne behind her; the High Priest waiting at the door of the temple, hands extended to receive her; angels above.
- **Iconographic source:** Protoevangelium of James, chapters 7–8. The narrative: Joachim and Anne, having miraculously conceived Mary in their old age (the Protoevangelium opens with their long barrenness, paralleling Hannah and Sarah), vow to dedicate her to God. At age 3 they bring her to the Jerusalem temple. The High Priest (in some traditions, Zechariah, the future father of John the Baptist) receives her and — in an extra-biblical and theologically dubious detail — brings her into the Holy of Holies. Mary lives in the temple precinct until age 12, fed by angels, when she is betrothed to Joseph by lot.
- **Apocryphal status.** The Protoevangelium of James was widely read in the Byzantine Christian world but was rejected as canonical at all major councils. The Jerome / Vulgate tradition (Western Catholic) downgraded it; the Eastern Orthodox tradition liturgically celebrates the Entry of the Theotokos as one of the Twelve Great Feasts (November 21) but does not treat the Protoevangelium itself as scripture. Protestant tradition rejects both the narrative and the feast.
- **Date:** c. 1100. Same Komnenian-era mosaic program as the Daphni Pantocrator (#4 in our corpus).
- **Medium:** Wall mosaic on gold ground, in a squinch (architectural transition between the square base and the round dome above).
- **Location:** Daphni Monastery, Chaidari, near Athens, Greece. UNESCO World Heritage Site (1990, with Hosios Loukas and Nea Moni). Same site as #4 Daphni Pantocrator. Approx. 38.0136° N, 23.6358° E.
- **Era:** Middle Byzantine.
- **Period:** Middle Byzantine, Komnenian.

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- **The temple-Holy-of-Holies-Mary connection.** Byzantine theological tradition reads Mary's Protoevangelium childhood-in-the-temple as iconographically/theologically pointing toward her body becoming the new Holy of Holies. The High Priest who receives her at the temple steps will be replaced by Christ as the high priest who enters his own body-temple (Hebrews 9:11–12). The reflection extracts this canonical doctrine (Mary's body as new temple, via Christ being the new temple and being formed in her womb) without endorsing the apocryphal narrative.
- **John 2:19–21 as the canonical anchor.** "Destroy this temple..." is Christ's own self-identification with the temple. The phrase "but he spake of the temple of his body" (v. 21) is the Johannine clarification that makes the body-temple identification explicit. The Christ-as-temple doctrine, then read backwards through Mary's nine-month carrying of Christ, gives Mary-as-temple-bearer without the Protoevangelium narrative being needed.
- **Hebrews 9 typological framework.** Hebrews 9:1–12 establishes the Old Testament tabernacle/temple as the type fulfilled in Christ's body. The reflection draws on this framework even though Hebrews 9 is not quoted directly.
