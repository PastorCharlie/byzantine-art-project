## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Mosaic from the program of votive panels in the Basilica of Hagios Demetrios at Thessaloniki, late 6th–7th century. The image used represents the broader Demetrios votive program; the basilica's mosaics include several panels showing Saint Demetrios with bishops, civic officials, children, and other Thessalonian recipients of his patronage.
- **Date:** Late 6th–7th century. The basilica's earliest mosaic phase is 5th century; the surviving votive program is largely from the rebuilding after the 620 earthquake (and after additional fires). Some panels date from the late 6th c.; the bulk is 7th c. (post-620). The famous fires of 1917 destroyed many mosaics; survivors include several pre-iconoclasm votive panels.
- **Saint Demetrios history:** Roman officer in Thessaloniki, executed c. 306 AD under Diocletian/Maximian/Galerius for openly preaching Christ to a city under imperial pagan-only orders. Speared to death (or stabbed) in the imperial bath-house complex on which the basilica was later built. The Christians of Thessaloniki built a small chapel over his grave by the 4th c.; the great basilica was begun in the 5th c. and rebuilt repeatedly through fires, earthquakes, and Slavic invasions.
- **Medium:** Wall mosaic on gold ground. The basilica's surviving 6th–7th c. mosaics constitute one of the most important pre-iconoclasm Byzantine mosaic programs outside of Constantinople (which lost most of its pre-iconoclasm work to iconoclasm and to the Fourth Crusade).
- **Location:** Basilica of Hagios Demetrios (Greek: Ἅγιος Δημήτριος), Thessaloniki, northern Greece. UNESCO World Heritage Site (1988, "Paleochristian and Byzantine Monuments of Thessalonika"). Same UNESCO inscription as Hagia Sophia Thessaloniki (#37). Approx. 40.6386° N, 22.9469° E.
- **Era:** Early Byzantine.
- **Period:** Early Byzantine, post-620 reconstruction.

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- **Votive iconography.** The Hagios Demetrios mosaics are *votive* — commissioned by individuals or communities thanking Demetrios for specific protections (deliverance from Slavic raids in 615 and 617; healing from plague; rescue of captured children; etc.). This makes the mosaic program the corpus's clearest example of saint-veneration-as-protection-cult, requiring the locked-in Coll 7 framing rule's full discipline.
- **Cultic context.** Hagios Demetrios was historically the most important saint-cult site in Greece and one of the most important in the Eastern Roman Empire after Constantinople. Demetrios served as the city's protector — invoked against Slavic invasions, Bulgar raids, plague, fire. The reflection acknowledges this cultic history while declining to participate in it.
- **Coll 7 framing rule applied with full discipline.** The reflection: (1) names the historical Demetrios, (2) describes the votive iconography honestly, (3) declines cultic veneration explicitly, (4) reframes the saint via Hebrews 11–12 cloud-of-witnesses + Acts 4:19–20 apostolic-obedience pattern, (5) closing imperative directs readers to imitation, not intercession.
- **John 18:9 protection-of-the-church anchor.** "Of them which thou gavest me have I lost none." Christ's own statement of his protective work over the church. This is the GLM-Coll-7 alternative to the "Demetrios protects the city" narrative — Christ protects his people; the saints witness to this protection.
