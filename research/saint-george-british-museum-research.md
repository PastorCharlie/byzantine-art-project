## Quick reference

- **Object:** 13th-c. Byzantine icon, "St George and the Youth of Mytilene." British Museum 1984.0601.1. Tempera on linen on pine. 18.8 × 26.8 cm. CC BY 2.5 (Marie-Lan Nguyen 2012). 2531×3376 px.
- **Date:** c. 1250 (post-Fourth-Crusade, early Palaeologan).
- **Subject:** Mytilene-rescue legend, NOT the dragon legend.

## Calibration decisions

- **Mode 4 / dragon-rejection.** The dragon legend is medieval (11th-c. Georgian text earliest; Western *Golden Legend* 1260s). The Mytilene-rescue legend (which the icon actually shows) is also legendary. Both declined.
- **Historical core affirmed:** George of Lydda, Roman soldier-martyr under Diocletian c. 303. Same persecution wave as #43 Sergius and Bacchus, #46 Forty Martyrs.
- **Christological redirect: Revelation 12:7–11.** Pastor Charlie's directional note for George was the right move. The dragon-slayer in canonical scripture is the Lamb, not a knight. George belongs in the *"they overcame him by the blood of the Lamb, and by the word of their testimony"* line — testimony-army, not solo-dragon-slayer. The icon's iconography "got the species right (the dragon is real) and the figure wrong."
- **Named-decline applied:** *"We do not pray to Saint George. We do not invoke him as a battle-saint or a national patron — England, Russia, Georgia, Ethiopia, and others have done so for centuries; we decline."*
- **Sources:** Wikipedia "Saint George"; Wikipedia "Saint George and the Dragon"; standard hagiographic-historical assessments.
