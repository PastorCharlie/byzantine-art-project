## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Glazed ceramic icon tile of Saint Nicholas, 10th-c. Constantinople workshop. Square panel approximately 16.7 × 16.4 × 0.8 cm. Standard Byzantine bishop iconography: white *omophorion* (bishop's stole) with crosses; right hand raised in benediction; left hand holds a gospel-book; eyes meet the viewer; Greek inscription naming the saint.
- **Date:** c. 950. Middle Byzantine, Macedonian Renaissance period.
- **Medium:** Glazed ceramic tile. Likely produced in a Constantinople workshop and used as part of an architectural decorative frieze (attached to a church wall, perhaps grouped with similar saint-tiles in a continuous program).
- **Location:** Walters Art Museum, Baltimore, Maryland (accession 48.2086.1). Approx. 39.2965° N, –76.6160° W. Originally produced in Constantinople; current location reflects 19th-c. or 20th-c. acquisition by an American collector.
- **Era:** Middle Byzantine.
- **Period:** Middle Byzantine, Macedonian Renaissance.

## 2. Saint Nicholas — historical core vs. legendary accretions

**Historically defensible (per Wikipedia consensus and conservative historiography):**
- Bishop of Myra in Lycia (modern Demre, southern Turkey).
- Born c. 270 AD, died c. 343 AD.
- Probably imprisoned during Diocletian's persecution (303–311).
- Attended Council of Nicaea, 325 AD — his name appears on some surviving lists of attending bishops.
- Buried at Myra; his relics were later translated to Bari, Italy (1087, by Italian sailors), where they remain.

**Legendary accretions (declined in reflection):**
- **Slapping Arius at Nicaea** — late legend, no contemporary attestation. The conciliar acts do not record it. First documented in Byzantine hagiography centuries later.
- **Resurrecting three murdered children pickled in a butcher's brine-tub** — 12th-century French embellishment, grotesque details inconsistent with earlier hagiography.
- **Saving sailors from storms** — generic post-mortem-miracle hagiography, no contemporary attestation.
- **The bag-of-gold-through-the-window story (saving three girls from prostitution)** — older than the others, possibly historical at the core (the early *Vita* by Michael the Archimandrite, c. 9th c., includes it), but every dramatic flourish in modern retellings cannot be verified.
- **The entire Santa Claus mythology** — post-Reformation Western conflation of Saint Nicholas with Germanic folklore figures, finalized in 19th-c. American popular culture (Clement Clarke Moore's poem, Coca-Cola advertising). Has no relationship to the historical bishop.
