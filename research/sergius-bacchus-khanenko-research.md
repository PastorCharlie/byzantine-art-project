## 1. Identification

- **Object:** Encaustic icon depicting Saints Sergius and Bacchus standing frontally side by side. Iconographic features:
  - **Sergius (left, viewer's left)** — wearing the *chlamys* (military cloak) and the gold *maniakion* (military collar — a torque-like neck-piece marking high Roman military rank)
  - **Bacchus (right, viewer's right)** — same military attire
  - **Christ in roundel** at the top center, between the two saints' heads — the iconographic focal point of the composition
  - Frontal hieratic pose; both saints face the viewer directly
- **Medium:** Encaustic on wooden panel (beech wood per scholarly description). Same medium as the Sinai Pantocrator (#2) and Sinai Virgin Enthroned (#24).
- **Dimensions:** Approximately 28 × 41 cm. Small portable panel.
- **Date:** 6th–7th century. Some scholarship narrows to early 7th c.; broader consensus is the 580–700 window.
- **Provenance:** Originally from Saint Catherine's Monastery, Sinai. Transferred to the Ecclesiastic Academy of Kyiv in the 19th century by Archimandrite Porfiry Uspensky (1804–1885), an Orthodox cleric who acquired it from Sinai during his expeditions. Moved to the Kyiv Central Antireligious Museum after the Russian Revolution (1917+), then to the Khanenko Museum where it currently resides.
- **Current location:** Bohdan and Varvara Khanenko National Museum of Arts, Kyiv, Ukraine. Approx. 50.4378° N, 30.5184° E.
- **War context:** The Khanenko Museum's icon collection (including this Sergius and Bacchus panel) was secretly evacuated from Kyiv in 2023 during the Russia-Ukraine war and exhibited at the Louvre in Paris (June 2023) for protective custody, in an exhibition titled "At the Origins of the Sacred Image: Icons from the Bohdan and Varvara Khanenko National Museum of Arts in Kyiv." Current physical location may vary; the Khanenko remains the official institutional holder.
- **Era:** Early Byzantine.
- **Period:** Early Byzantine, pre-iconoclasm.

## 2. The saints' history

- **Historical core:** Sergius and Bacchus were Roman military officers serving in the *schola gentilium* (a unit of provincial military forces). They held high rank — the *primicerius* and the *secundicerius* of the unit. During the reign of Maximian (co-emperor with Diocletian, r. 286–305), they refused an order to sacrifice to Jupiter, were stripped of rank, paraded through the streets in women's clothing as public humiliation, and tortured. Bacchus was scourged to death first; Sergius was beheaded later, c. 303 AD. The historical core is reasonably well-attested in the early martyrologies.
- **Cultic veneration:** Sergius and Bacchus became major Eastern saints, patrons of the Byzantine military. Justinian built the Church of Saints Sergius and Bacchus in Constantinople (now Küçük Ayasofya Mosque) c. 530 — the so-called "Little Hagia Sophia." Major cult centers at Resafa (Syria, where Sergius's relics were enshrined) and elsewhere.
- **Modern interpretive contestation:** John Boswell's *Same-Sex Unions in Premodern Europe* (1994) argued Sergius and Bacchus were a romantic couple based on the Greek *adelphopoiesis* ("brother-making") liturgical tradition that included them. This claim is contested by mainstream historians; the *adelphopoiesis* rite is generally read as a brotherhood ritual rather than a marriage analog. The reflection deliberately stays out of this contemporary debate — engaging it would distract from the Mode-4 work the reflection actually does (affirming their faith under persecution; declining cultic veneration).
