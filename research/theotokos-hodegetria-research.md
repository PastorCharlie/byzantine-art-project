## 1. Identification — type and instance

This entry is unusual in that it represents an iconographic **type** rather than a single physical artwork. The BRIEF.md §4 Coll 2 #3 entry is "Theotokos Hodegetria, original tradition — multiple surviving copies." We treat this entry as a representative reflection on the Hodegetria type, illustrated by a specific 13th-century surviving example (the Byzantine and Christian Museum, Athens icon).

### The type

- **Hodegetria (Greek ὁδηγήτρια, *hodēgētria*):** "she who shows the way" / "guide." The iconographic type depicts the Theotokos holding the Christ-child on her left arm while her right hand makes a gesture of presentation toward him.
- **The gesture.** Mary's right hand extends across her body, palm facing the Christ-child. The gesture is iconographically distinct from the *Eleousa* (cheek-to-cheek tenderness) and *Enthroned* (formal frontal majesty) types: in those, Mary's hands are occupied holding/embracing the Child; in the Hodegetria, the right hand is freed to point.
- **The legendary original.** The prototype was kept at the Monastery of the Panagia Hodegoi (Ἡ Παναγία τῶν Ὁδηγῶν, "the All-Holy of the Guides") in Constantinople. Tradition attributed it to Saint Luke (an attribution rejected by serious scholarship — the icon was probably 5th–6th century). The monastery is named for the *Hodegoi*, the spring whose waters were said to heal blindness; Mary the *Hodegētria* is "she who guides the blind." The original icon was processed weekly through Constantinople and was the empire's premier Palladium.
- **Destruction.** The original was destroyed when the Ottomans took Constantinople on 29 May 1453. Some traditions claim it was smuggled out; no consensus among modern historians on whether any surviving icon is the original. The *Hodegētria* type survives via a flood of copies — hundreds of pre-modern copies, thousands of post-modern.
- **Iconographic spread.** The Hodegetria became the most-copied iconographic type in Eastern Christian visual history. Major surviving Byzantine examples: at Sinai (Saint Catherine's), at Athos (multiple monasteries), at Hilandar (Serbian, on Athos), at Cyprus, at the Tretyakov in Moscow (multiple), at the Walters Art Museum, at the Cambrai Cathedral (medieval Western copy), and many more.

### The instance shown in the corpus image

- **Object:** 13th-century Byzantine icon, Hodegetria type, currently held at the Byzantine and Christian Museum (Βυζαντινό και Χριστιανικό Μουσείο), Athens, Greece.
- **Date:** 13th century — late-Byzantine, possibly Crusader-period or post-1261.
- **Medium:** Tempera and gold leaf on wooden panel.
- **Why this example:** The Athens icon is well-photographed under permissive license (Giovanni Dall'Orto 2009), is a clean exemplar of the Hodegetria iconographic conventions, and survives in a museum context (rather than active liturgical/veneration use), which suits our archive's engagement with theological content over veneration practice. Other strong candidates (the Cambrai Madonna, Korsunskaya Hodegetria at the Kremlin, Sinai Hodegetrias) are licensing-encumbered or less well-photographed.

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- **Iconographic gesture as theological argument.** The right hand pointing at Christ is the entire content of the type. Belting (1994) and Pentcheva (2006) both argue that the Hodegetria iconography is structurally Christological even as its devotional reception became increasingly Marian over time. This is theologically usable for the GLM-distinctive framing — the icon's own logic is Christ-directing.
- **Hodegetria vs. later Marian devotion.** The historical irony: the Hodegetria was the most beloved Byzantine Marian image, processed weekly, credited with miracles, the empire's protector. Yet its iconography says "look at him, not me." Pentcheva's book is largely a historical study of how the *visual* Christological pointing got overlaid by *devotional* Marian focus. The reflection makes use of this irony.
- **Saint Luke attribution.** Tradition attributes the original to Saint Luke. This is not historically defensible (Luke was a 1st-century evangelist; the original Hodegetria is at earliest 5th–6th c.). Most scholars treat the Lukan attribution as a pious legend asserting the icon's authentic apostolic origin without literal historical claim. The reflection mentions the tradition without endorsing or critiquing it; it's contextually relevant but not theologically load-bearing.
- **1453 destruction.** Confirmed by multiple Byzantine and Ottoman sources: when Mehmet II took Constantinople on 29 May 1453, the original Hodegetria was hacked apart by Janissaries who valued the silver and gilded panel covers. Some sources say it was burned. Various Western European icons claim to be either the smuggled original or fragments of it; none has scholarly consensus.
