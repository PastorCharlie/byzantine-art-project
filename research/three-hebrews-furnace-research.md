## 1. Identification

- **Object:** A 16th-century post-Byzantine Levantine icon depicting Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego in the fiery furnace of Daniel 3. Composition: three young men standing in a stylized furnace, hands raised in *orant* (praying) posture, flames around but not touching their bodies, a fourth figure (smaller, sometimes haloed) among or beside them, sometimes Nebuchadnezzar visible looking on from outside the furnace.
- **Date:** c. 1550 (16th century). Post-Byzantine, Levantine (Lebanese/Syrian) provenance. The icon belongs to the Eastern Christian iconographic tradition that continued after the fall of Constantinople (1453); Levantine post-Byzantine icons are theologically and iconographically continuous with the Byzantine tradition.
- **Provenance note:** This image is part of the "One Hundred Icon Project" by the Wikimedians of the Levant. Specific museum/church location not specified in the source.
- **Medium:** Tempera and pigments on wooden panel (typical for Levantine post-Byzantine icons).
- **Era:** Post-Byzantine (this specific instance). The iconographic type itself (Three Hebrews in the Furnace) goes back to early Christian art — the type appears in 3rd–4th century Roman catacomb paintings (Catacombs of Priscilla) and was preserved through Byzantine and post-Byzantine traditions continuously.
- **BRIEF context:** BRIEF Coll 6 #7 lists "The Three Hebrews in the Furnace, various — Christ-as-fourth-figure." The "various" framing accepts post-Byzantine instances; the "Christ-as-fourth-figure" descriptor describes the iconographic claim that this reflection rejects.

Key contextual facts that shaped the reflection:
- **The Aramaic *bar elahin* problem.** Daniel 3:25 in the original Aramaic reads "*bar-elahin*" (בַּר־אֱלָהִין). This compound noun can be translated as either "a son of the gods" (taking *elahin* as a polytheistic plural — Nebuchadnezzar speaking from his pagan worldview) or "the Son of God" (taking *elahin* as a plural of majesty referring to the one true God). The KJV chose "Son of God"; modern translations are mixed (NIV "son of the gods"; ESV "son of the gods"; NASB "son of the gods"). The Christian-tradition reading (Christ-as-fourth) depends on the KJV's chosen translation; the more probable reading given Nebuchadnezzar's pagan context is "son of the gods." This is significant because the Christ-as-fourth typology rests partly on a translation choice.
- **Daniel 3:28 self-correction.** Three verses after the contested *bar-elahin* line, Nebuchadnezzar himself identifies the fourth figure as "his angel" (Aramaic *malakhe*, מַלְאֲכֵהּ). This is the canonical text's authoritative identification of the fourth figure. The reflection treats Daniel 3:28 as decisive: the canonical text says angel; the typological tradition says Christ; the canonical text wins.
- **Hebrews 11:34 as the NT-affirmed reading.** "Quenched the violence of fire" (Heb 11:34) is the apostolic identification of the Daniel 3 event in the cloud-of-witnesses framework. The three Hebrews are saints; the fire was quenched; faith was the means. The NT does not say the fourth figure was Christ. This is the textbook case of the locked-in Coll 6 rule — affirm what the NT affirms (faith-witness), reject what the NT does not affirm (Christ-as-fourth).
- **Daniel 3:17–18 "But if not."** The most famous line in the Daniel 3 narrative is the three's reply to Nebuchadnezzar: "our God whom we serve is able to deliver us... but if not, be it known unto thee, O king, that we will not serve thy gods" (vv. 17–18). This is the doctrinal weight of the scene — faith without guarantee. The iconography stages this faith-position; the reflection extracts it as the canonical-affirmed teaching.

This is the corpus's first reflection that explicitly REJECTS a major iconographic typology while continuing to engage the artwork charitably. The pattern:
1. **Acknowledge what the iconography is doing** (Christ-as-fourth tradition; KJV's "Son of God" translation; Patristic developers).
2. **Read the canonical text more carefully** (Aramaic *bar-elahin* alternative; Daniel 3:28's self-correction to "angel").
3. **State the locked-in rule** ("we affirm a type only when a New Testament author affirmed it first").
4. **Identify what the NT actually affirms** (Hebrews 11:34 faith-quenched-fire).
5. **Pastoral application** ("the doctrine in this passage is faithfulness, and the doctrine survives without overreach").
6. **Closing imperative directed at saints-as-faith-witnesses, not Christ-as-fourth-figure**.

This is the model for handling iconographic typologies the corpus cannot affirm (Marian-bush at #39 was a partial example; this reflection is the fuller execution).
