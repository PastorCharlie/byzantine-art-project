## Quick reference

- **Object:** 15th-c. Russian (Novgorod-school) icon depicting Theodore Stratelates and Theodore Tyron paired. Hermitage hall 146. CC BY-SA 4.0 (Netelo 2020). 2674×3824 px.
- **Subjects:** Theodore Tyron (4th-c. recruit-martyr from Amasea, Pontus); Theodore Stratelates (4th-c. general-martyr from Heraclea).
- **Both** executed under Licinius for refusing pagan sacrifice.

## Calibration decisions

- **Historical core affirmed for both Theodores.** Real soldier-martyrs of the Diocletianic-Licinian persecutions, executed for refusing imperial pagan cult.
- **Legendary embellishments declined.** Theodore Tyron's temple-burning of Cybele's shrine; Theodore Stratelates's idol-destruction, crucifixion, miraculous preservation, beheading. These are dramatic later accretions; the simple martyrdom-for-refusing-cult is the historical core.
- **Distinguishing angle (different from #43 Sergius/Bacchus and #46 Forty Martyrs):** the *plural* shape of Christian witness. The Theodores are painted together because martyrdom is rarely solitary. Revelation 12:11's *"they overcame him"* uses Greek *autoi* (plural) — *they* overcame, not *he* overcame. The army of testifiers is plural by design.
- **Iconographic note developed:** the Byzantine convention shows soldier-martyrs *with weapons* — a deliberate choice. The point is not that swords saved them; the point is that even men trained for violence were called to refuse the violence the empire demanded. The icon's swords are symbolic of training; martyrdom is the witness that their training served Christ rather than Caesar.
- **Named-decline applied:** *"We do not pray to the Theodores. We do not invoke them as patrons of soldiers, of civic protection, or of any other category that the Byzantine cult assigned them."*
- **Closing imperative:** *"Lay it down for Christ."* — the soldier-saints' actual witness was laying down what their weapons were trained to defend.
